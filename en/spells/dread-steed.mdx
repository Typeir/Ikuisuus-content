# Dread Steed

A spell that assembles a temporary mount from a carcass and its own severed bones, forced into motion by binding magic and momentum.  
The steed is neither illusion nor resurrection: its flesh is inert, its bones suspended and locked into place only while the spell endures.  
When the magic fades, the structure collapses, leaving nothing but scattered remains.

---

> **Dread Steed**  
> _3rd-level Necromancy (Ritual)_  
> **Casting Time**: 1 minute  
> **Range**: 30 feet  
> **Components**: V, S, M (the corpse of a Large quadruped or a similar creature)  
> **Duration**: Concentration, up 1 hour  
>
> You assemble a Large skeletal mount from the provided corpse in an unoccupied space you can see within range.  
> The steed’s bones lift free from the carcass and lock into a running configuration, held together by binding force rather than life.
>
> For the duration, you or a creature you choose can ride the steed.  
> The steed uses the statistics of a **riding horse**, except it has a speed of **70 feet** and can travel **7 miles in an hour**, or **9 miles** at a fast pace, and has 10 hit points.
> The steed is immune to poison and psychic damage, as well as the poisoned,  and frightened conditions.
>
> Any saddle, reins, or tack formed as part of the steed collapse into loose bone fragments if carried more than **10 feet** away from it.
>
> **Trample.**  
> While mounted, you may use your **action** to force the steed to surge forward up to **20 feet** in a straight line through a creature.  
> That creature must succeed on a **Strength saving throw** or take **4d8 bludgeoning damage** and be **knocked prone**.
>
> After using Trample, you immediately **dismount**, landing in an unoccupied space within **5 feet** of the target, and the steed **collapses and falls apart**, ending the spell.
>
> When the spell ends normally, the steed loses cohesion over **1 minute**, allowing its rider time to dismount safely before the bones scatter.  
> The spell also ends if you use an action to dismiss it or if the steed dies.
