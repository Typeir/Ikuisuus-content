# Monster Types and Basics

## Overview

This section describes common creature types, how a monster's alignment is used, and basic mechanical values printed in a stat block (Armor Class, Hit Points, Tags, Size). It explains which creatures are suitable as player characters and which are hostile or unaligned by nature.

## Creature Types and Notes

- Giants
  - Giants are generally humanlike in shape and often larger than humans. Varieties commonly listed include hill giants, stone giants, frost giants, fire giants, cloud giants, and storm giants. Some giant-variant creatures (for example, ettins or fomorians) present additional heads or deformities; tags such as "demon" may appear on unusual variants produced by other origins. Ogres and trolls are often treated as giant kin in many bestiaries.

- Humanoids
  - Humanoids are the primary peoples of the setting: humans, dwarves, elves, halflings, and many other playable races. They have language and culture, few innate magical abilities (though they can learn spellcasting), and a bipedal form. Humanoids are the races most suitable for player characters.

- Goblinoids, Orcs, Gnolls, Kobolds, Lizardfolk
  - These constitute families of humanoid or near-humanoid races often described as savage or tribal. They are typically listed separately when a monster's entry needs additional categorization beyond its type.

- Monstrosities
  - Monstrosities are creatures formed by unnatural processes or terrible curses, and they defy ordinary biological classification (examples include minotaurs and yuan-ti). They are often hostile and are a catch-all for creatures that do not fit other types.

- Oozes
  - Oozes are gelatinous creatures that rarely have a fixed shape. They are mostly subterranean, dwell in caves and dungeons, feed on refuse and carrion, and are unaligned; they do not make moral choices. Black puddings and gelatinous cubes are common examples.

- Plants
  - In this context, Plants are vegetable creatures rather than ordinary flora. Most are ambulatory and some are carnivorous. Notable examples include the shambling mound and the treant. Fungal creatures such as gas spores and myconids may also be classed here.

- Undead
  - Undead are once-living creatures brought to a horrifying state of undeath by necromantic magic or foul curses. This category includes walking corpses such as zombies and vampires, and bodiless spirits such as ghosts and specters.

## Alignment

- A monster’s alignment entry in a stat block provides a clue to its disposition and likely behaviour in roleplaying or combat situations. For example, a chaotic evil monster might attack characters on sight, while a neutral monster might be willing to negotiate.
- The alignment shown in a stat block is the default; the GM is free to change a monster’s alignment to suit the needs of the campaign. If a specific creature type can be any alignment, the entry or text will say so (for example, a berserker might be any chaotic alignment).
- Many low‑intelligence creatures lack comprehension of law/chaos and good/evil and thus do not make moral or ethical choices; such creatures are treated as unaligned.

## Armor Class (AC)

- A monster that wears armor or carries a shield has an Armor Class (AC) that reflects the protection of that armor and shield and incorporates its Dexterity modifier where appropriate.
- If a monster has natural armor, wears armor, or carries a shield, that fact is noted parenthetically after its AC value in the stat block. For instance: "AC 15 (chain mail and shield)" or "AC 12 (natural armor)".
- Unless otherwise specified, a monster’s AC is based on its Dexterity modifier and natural armor where applicable.

## Hit Points

- A monster’s hit points are presented both as a dice expression (Hit Dice) and as an average number. For example, a monster with 2d8 hit points would have an average of 9 hit points (2 × 4½), and the stat block presents both the dice expression and the average value.
- The die used to calculate hit points is determined by the monster’s size. See the Hit Dice by Size table in the core rules for the precise die assigned to each size category.

## Tags and Size

- A monster might have one or more tags appended to its type in parentheses to provide additional categorization (for example, an orc might be listed as "humanoid (orc)" type). These parenthetical tags are descriptive and have no rules of their own unless explicitly called out elsewhere.
- A monster’s size determines the die used to calculate its hit points; refer to the Hit Dice by Size table for the mapping from size to hit die.

## Mechanics (reproduction of presented mechanics and presentation rules)

- Stat blocks present common mechanical fields: Size, Type (with parenthetical tags), Alignment, Armor Class, Hit Points (dice expression and average), Speed, Ability Scores, Saving Throws, Skills, Damage Vulnerabilities/Resistances/Immunities, Condition Immunities, Senses, Languages, Challenge, and any proficiencies or special tags.
- The default alignment printed in a stat block is only a guideline; it can be altered by the GM for narrative reasons.
- Creatures with no alignment do not make moral decisions and are functionally unaligned for all mechanical purposes.
- Oozes are explicitly unaligned and are typically subterranean scavengers; plants are vegetable creatures that may be ambulatory and sometimes carnivorous; undead are created by necromancy and include both corporeal and incorporeal forms.

## Interactions

- Many entries reference other systems (for example, specific resistances, immunities, or condition interactions). When an effect or ability references spellcasting, damage types, or conditions, resolve those using the usual rules for spells, damage, and conditions.
- If a creature’s type or tags imply a particular interaction (for example, a demon tag interacting with effects that target fiends), consult that effect’s rules for exact behaviour.

## References and Notes

- The Hit Dice by Size table and the canonical mappings of Size → hit die are part of the core rules and should be consulted for exact numeric behaviour when calculating average hit points from Hit Dice.
- The categories and examples above are intended to clarify how creatures are described and presented in stat blocks; specific monsters may include additional details, special traits, or exceptions in their individual stat blocks.
