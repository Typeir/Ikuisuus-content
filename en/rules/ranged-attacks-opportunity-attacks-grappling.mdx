# Ranged Attacks, Opportunity Attacks, and Grappling

## Overview

This page collects the combat rules that govern ranged attacks, opportunity attacks, two-weapon fighting, ranged attacks made in close combat, melee attacks, and grappling. It explains when attacks provoke opportunity attacks, how range and reach affect attack rolls, the special cases for two-weapon fighting and thrown weapons, and the full procedure for grappling and escaping a grapple.

## Mechanics

### Opportunity Attacks

- You can make an opportunity attack when a hostile creature that you can see moves out of your reach.
- To make the opportunity attack, you use your reaction to make one melee attack against the provoking creature.
- The opportunity attack occurs immediately before the creature leaves your reach.
- You can avoid provoking an opportunity attack by taking the Disengage action.
- You also don’t provoke an opportunity attack when you teleport or when someone or something moves you without using your movement, action, or reaction. For example, an explosion that hurls you out of a foe’s reach or a force that causes you to fall past an enemy does not provoke an opportunity attack.

### Ranged Attacks and Range

- You can make ranged attacks only against targets within a specified range.
- Some ranged attacks (for example, those made with a longbow or a shortbow) have two ranges: the smaller number is the weapon’s normal range, and the larger number is its long range.
  - When you attack a target beyond normal range but within long range, your attack roll has disadvantage.
  - You can’t attack a target beyond the long range.
- If a ranged attack (such as a spell or a thrown weapon) has a single range, you can’t attack a target beyond that range.

### Ranged Attacks in Close Combat

- Aiming a ranged attack is more difficult when a foe is next to you.
- When you make a ranged attack with a weapon, a spell, or some other means, you have disadvantage on the attack roll if you are within 5 feet of a hostile creature that can see you and that creature isn’t incapacitated.
- If either weapon you are wielding has the thrown property, you can throw that weapon instead of making a melee attack with it.

### Two-Weapon Fighting

- When you take the Attack action and attack with a light melee weapon that you’re holding in one hand, you can use a bonus action to attack with a different light melee weapon that you’re holding in the other hand.
- You don’t add your ability modifier to the damage of the bonus attack, unless that modifier is negative.
- If either weapon has the thrown property, you can throw the weapon instead of making a melee attack with it.

### Melee Attacks

- A melee attack is used in hand-to-hand combat and allows you to attack a foe within your reach.
- A melee attack typically uses a handheld weapon such as a sword, warhammer, or an axe, but many monsters make melee attacks with claws, horns, teeth, tentacles, or other body parts.
- A typical creature has 5-foot reach and can thus attack targets within 5 feet when making a melee attack. Some larger creatures have greater reach as noted in their descriptions.
- Instead of using a weapon to make a melee weapon attack, you can use an unarmed strike: a punch, kick, head-butt, or similar forceful blow.
  - An unarmed strike deals bludgeoning damage equal to 1 + your Strength modifier.
  - You are proficient with your unarmed strikes.

### Grappling

- When you want to grab a creature or wrestle with it, you can use the Attack action to make a special melee attack, a grapple.
- If you are able to make multiple attacks with the Attack action, the grapple attack replaces one of them.
- The target of your grapple must be no more than one size larger than you and must be within your reach.
- Using at least one free hand, you try to seize the target by making a grapple check instead of an attack roll: a Strength (Athletics) check contested by the target’s choice of either Strength (Athletics) or Dexterity (Acrobatics).
  - If you succeed, you subject the target to the grappled condition (see the conditions appendix). A grappled creature’s speed becomes 0, and it can’t benefit from any bonus to its speed.
  - You can release a grapple whenever you like (no action required).

#### Escaping a Grapple

- A grappled creature can use its action to escape.
- To escape, the grappled creature must succeed on a Strength (Athletics) or Dexterity (Acrobatics) check contested by the grappler’s Strength (Athletics) check.
- If the grappled creature succeeds, it is no longer grappled.

## Interactions

- Disengage prevents opportunity attacks but does not prevent being grappled by a reach attack that doesn’t provoke (reach depends on the attacker).
- Teleportation and forced movement that does not use the target’s movement, action, or reaction do not provoke opportunity attacks.
- Spells that require ranged attack rolls follow the same range and disadvantage rules as weapon attacks; if a spell’s description gives a single range, that single range is absolute.
- The thrown property interacts with two-weapon fighting and with melee attacks: a weapon with the thrown property can be thrown instead of used for a melee attack, and the rules for two-weapon fighting apply normally when both weapons are light (or when other abilities modify those requirements).
