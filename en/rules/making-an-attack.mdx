# Making an Attack

## Overview

This rule governs how attack rolls are made and resolved: who you can target, which modifiers apply, what happens on a hit or miss, and special cases such as natural 1s/20s, unseen attackers and targets, and spell attacks that require attack rolls.

## Mechanics

### Attack Rolls

When you make an attack, roll a d20 and add the appropriate modifiers. If the total equals or exceeds the target's Armor Class (AC), the attack hits. On a hit, roll the attack's damage unless a specific attack says otherwise; some attacks cause special effects instead of or in addition to damage.

- Roll a d20 and add modifiers.
- Compare the total to the target's AC; if the total is greater than or equal to the AC, the attack hits.
- On a hit, resolve damage and any special effects listed for the attack.

Some attacks (including some spells) require an attack roll. The ability modifier used for a melee weapon attack is Strength, and the ability modifier used for a ranged weapon attack is Dexterity. Weapons that have the finesse or thrown property can use the alternative ability modifier provided by those properties. The ability modifier used for a spell attack is determined by the spellcasting ability of the caster.

Monsters make attack rolls using whatever modifier is provided in their stat block.

### Modifiers to the Roll

The two most common modifiers to an attack roll are an ability modifier and the character's proficiency bonus.

- Ability modifier: determined by the attack type (Strength for melee, Dexterity for ranged, or the spellcasting ability for spell attacks).
- Proficiency bonus: add your proficiency bonus to the attack roll when you are proficient with the weapon you are attacking with, and you also add your proficiency bonus to a spell attack when the rules for that spell or the character's class grant proficiency for spell attacks.

Other modifiers (cover, advantage/disadvantage, spells, special abilities, environmental effects) may apply as the GM determines.

### Resolve the Attack (Steps)

1. Choose a target. Pick a target within your attack's range: a creature, an object, or a location.
2. Determine modifiers. The GM determines whether the target has cover and whether you have advantage or disadvantage, and applies ability modifiers, proficiency, and other situational modifiers.
3. Resolve the attack. Roll the attack. On a hit, roll damage or apply the attack's specified effect. If an attack has special rules, follow those rules in place of or in addition to rolling damage.

### Rolling a 1 or 20

- Natural 20: If the d20 roll for an attack is a 20, the attack hits regardless of modifiers or the target's AC. This result is called a critical hit. Critical hits typically deal extra damage as specified by the attack or system rules.
- Natural 1: If the d20 roll for an attack is a 1, the attack misses regardless of modifiers or the target's AC.

### Unseen Attackers and Targets

- When you attack a target you can't see, you have disadvantage on the attack roll. This applies whether you're guessing the target's location or targeting a creature you can hear but not see.
- When a creature can’t see you, you have advantage on attack rolls against it.
- If the target isn’t in the location you targeted, the attack automatically misses; the GM usually simply rules that the attack missed rather than adjudicating whether the guess of location was correct.

Hidden and stealth interactions:

- A creature that is hidden—both unseen and unheard—gains the benefits of being hidden. If you make an attack while hidden, you give away your location when the attack hits or misses.

### Search and Perception while Attacking

- When you take the Search action, you devote attention to finding something. Depending on the nature of your search, the GM might call for a Wisdom (Perception) check or an Intelligence (Investigation) check.
- A combatant devoting attention to searching is not automatically attacking; the Search action is a distinct option the character can take on their turn.

### Use an Object

- You normally interact with an object while doing something else, such as drawing a sword as part of an attack. When an object requires your action for its use, take the Use an Object action.
- The Use an Object action is also useful when you want to interact with more than one object on your turn.

### Spell Attacks and Concentration Interaction

- Some spells require attack rolls; use the caster's spellcasting ability modifier for spell attack rolls (the spell or class determines which ability that is).
- Concentration and spellcasting interact with other actions: taking certain actions (for example, casting another spell that requires concentration or taking damage) can break concentration and end the ongoing spell effect. If your concentration is broken, the spell dissipates without taking effect.

## Interactions

- Advantage and disadvantage cancel each other: if you have both, they are ignored.
- Cover increases the target's effective AC as determined by the GM and applicable rules; always include cover when determining modifiers.
- Spells, features, and environmental effects can impose additional penalties or bonuses to attack rolls; apply those in step 2 of resolving the attack.
- Monster attack modifiers come from the monster's stat block and override generic character rules where the stat block specifies different values.
