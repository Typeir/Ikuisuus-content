# Movement and Space

## Overview

This section describes how creatures occupy space, how movement interacts with other creatures and objects, crawling and prone movement, squeezing, and special cases such as flying movement. It governs the area a creature controls in combat and the mechanical costs and limitations of moving through different kinds of terrain and spaces.

## Size and Space

A creature's space is the area, measured in feet, that it effectively controls in combat — not a literal measure of its physical dimensions. A typical Medium creature does not have to be literally 5 feet wide to control a 5-foot space; the space represents the area the creature can use to fight effectively. Because larger creatures take up more space, fewer of them can surround a creature.

| Creature Size | Space |
| --- | ---: |
| Tiny | 2½ by 2½ ft. |
| Small | 5 by 5 ft. |
| Medium | 5 by 5 ft. |
| Large | 10 by 10 ft. |
| Huge | 15 by 15 ft. |
| Gargantuan | 20 by 20 ft. or larger |

Examples of how a creature's space matters in play:

- If a Medium hobgoblin stands in a 5-foot-wide doorway, other creatures can't get through unless the hobgoblin lets them.  
- Assuming Medium combatants, eight creatures can fit in a 5-foot radius around another one; in contrast, as many as twenty Medium creatures can surround a Gargantuan one.  
- Because larger creatures take up more space, fewer of them can surround a creature.

## Interacting with Objects Around You

Certain simple interactions can be performed in tandem with your movement and action. A few examples of things you can do in tandem include:

- Draw or sheathe a sword
- Open or close a door
- Withdraw a potion from your backpack
- Pick up a dropped axe
- Take a bauble from a table
- Remove a ring from your finger
- Stuff some food into your mouth
- Plant a banner in the ground
- Fish a few coins from your belt pouch
- Drink all the ale in a flagon
- Throw a lever or a switch
- Pull a torch from a sconce
- Take a book from a shelf
- Extinguish a small flame
- Don a mask
- Pull the hood of your cloak up and over your head
- Put your ear to a door
- Kick a small stone
- Turn a key in a lock
- Tap the floor with a 10-foot pole
- Hand an item to another character

These are illustrative examples of the sorts of things you can do in the course of movement and action; a DM adjudicates similar simple interactions.

## Moving Around Other Creatures

- You can move through a nonhostile creature's space.  
- You can move through a hostile creature's space only if the creature is at least two sizes larger or smaller than you.  
- Whether a creature is a friend or an enemy, you can't willingly end your move in its space.  
- If you leave a hostile creature's reach during your move, you provoke an opportunity attack.

When a creature's space is smaller than the space you try to move through, moving through that other creature's space counts as difficult movement for you (see Squeezing, below) as determined by the DM.

## Squeezing into a Smaller Space

- A creature can squeeze through a space that is large enough for a creature one size smaller than it.  
- While squeezing through a smaller space, a creature's movement is hindered: it must spend 1 extra foot for every foot it moves there.  
- Attack rolls against a squeezing creature have advantage while it is in the smaller space.  
- The squeezing creature has disadvantage on attack rolls and on Dexterity saving throws while squeezed.  

These penalties reflect the awkwardness and restriction of the space; the DM decides when found openings qualify for squeezing.

## Crawling and Moving While Prone

- To move while prone, you must crawl or use magic such as teleportation.  
- Crawling costs 1 extra foot of movement for every foot moved (that is, crawling 1 foot costs an extra foot).  
- Crawling through difficult terrain is especially costly: crawling 1 foot in difficult terrain costs 3 feet of movement (the normal extra cost for crawling plus the extra cost imposed by difficult terrain).  
- If your speed is 0 while prone (for example, by being restrained or having 0 speed), you can't crawl or move normally.

Standing up from being prone requires movement: for example, if your speed is 30 feet, you must spend 15 feet of that movement to stand up. If you don't have enough movement left or if your speed is 0, you can't stand up (subject to DM adjudication and exceptions such as hovering or magical levitation).

## Difficult Terrain

- Difficult terrain slows movement. It increases the movement cost of each foot you move through it.  
- The combination of crawling and difficult terrain stacks: as noted above, crawling in difficult terrain can cost 3 feet of movement per foot moved (crawling's extra cost plus difficult terrain's cost).

## Flying Movement and Falling

- Flying creatures enjoy mobility benefits but must deal with the danger of falling.  
- If a flying creature is knocked prone, has its speed reduced to 0, or is otherwise deprived of the ability to move, the creature falls, unless it has the ability to hover or is being held aloft by magic (for example, by the fly spell).  
- A flying creature that is knocked prone or otherwise loses the ability to move falls immediately unless it can hover or some magic is keeping it aloft.  
- Standing up while flying follows the same movement-cost rules as standing up from prone on the ground; if you cannot pay the required movement to stand up (for example, you lack movement), you remain prone and, if unsupported, you fall.

## Interactions and DM Adjudication

- Many of these rules require on-the-spot adjudication by the DM, especially where object interaction, cramped spaces, and unusual terrain combine.  
- A creature's space represents the area it controls in combat, so the DM interprets movement, squeezing, and obstruction in light of both narrative and tactical concerns.
