# Diseases

## Overview

This section defines how diseases function as hazards and plot devices in play. Diseases can infect creatures through bites, contact with filth or contaminated material, or by exposure to afflicted individuals. Some diseases target specific creature types or races; others are broadly transmissible. A disease that affects more than a few party members is primarily a plot device — the rules below describe transmission, incubation, saving throws, symptom progression, recovery, and outbreak behaviour.

## Mechanics

### Transmission and Infection

- When a humanoid creature is bitten by a creature that carries a disease or when a creature comes into contact with filth or offal contaminated by a disease, the creature must succeed on a DC 11 Constitution saving throw or become infected.
- Some diseases are transmitted by particular vectors (for example, sewer-borne illnesses spread by rats and otyughs); other diseases might affect only constructs, undead, or a single race. Whether a disease can affect a creature is determined by the disease's description and the story the DM wants to tell.
- If an infected creature begins its turn within 10 feet of another creature in the throes of certain effects (for example, a fit of mad laughter described below), that creature may be required to make an additional saving throw to avoid infection. Example: any humanoid that starts its turn within 10 feet of an infected creature in the throes of mad laughter must succeed on a DC 10 Constitution saving throw or become infected with that disease.

### Incubation, Symptoms, and Effects

- Each disease entry specifies an incubation period (for example, 1d4 hours or 1d4 days) before symptoms manifest.
- Symptoms are mechanical: ability penalties, exhaustion levels, reduced healing from Hit Dice, vision penalties, damage, incapacitation, or other specified effects. Reproduce symptom lists exactly as provided by the disease entry.
- Exhaustion from disease is tracked using the normal exhaustion rules. Some diseases impose levels of exhaustion that cannot be removed by normal means until the disease is cured.
- Where a disease inflicts levels of exhaustion, track the creature's exhaustion normally; some disease entries specify how exhaustion can be reduced (see Recovery, below).

### Saving Throws and Progression

- Many diseases allow an infected creature a saving throw at the end of each long rest to resist worsening effects or to recover. Unless otherwise specified by the disease, use the save DC given in the disease entry.
- Example generic progression (if present in a disease entry): at the end of each long rest, an infected creature makes a DC 13 Constitution saving throw (or the DC specified by the disease). On a failed save, the character gains one level of exhaustion. On a successful save, the character's exhaustion level decreases by one. If a successful saving throw reduces the infected creature's level of exhaustion below 1, the creature recovers from the disease.
- If a disease describes a sequence of failing saves (for example, failing three of these saves), that sequence can result in lasting or indefinite effects (for example, a form of indefinite madness). Apply these consequences exactly as described by the disease entry.

### Outbreaks and Scale

- A simple outbreak is a small drain on party resources and is typically curable by Lesser Restoration or similar magic. A more complicated outbreak can form the basis of one or more adventures.
- The rules may provide a mechanic for the outbreak's severity (for example, saving throw DCs, incubation times, and symptom severity). The DM can alter these values for campaign needs, but do not change the structure of mechanical effects without noting the change.
- If an outbreak mechanic includes a DC that decreases over time (for example, a save DC that drops), follow the disease text; when the saving throw DC drops to 0 by the disease's progression rules, the creature recovers from the disease.

## Sample Diseases

The following sample diseases illustrate a variety of ways a disease can operate. Use them as written or alter their save DCs, incubation times, symptoms, and other characteristics to suit a campaign.

### Sewer Plague

- Incubation: 1d4 days before symptoms manifest.
- Transmission: common in sewers, refuse heaps, and stagnant swamps; transmitted by creatures that dwell in those areas (for example, rats and otyughs) or by contact with contaminated material.
- Symptoms: fatigue and cramps; when symptoms appear, the infected creature suffers one level of exhaustion.
- Healing penalty: the infected creature regains only half the normal number of hit points from spending Hit Dice and gains no hit points from finishing a long rest while infected.
- End of long rest save: At the end of each long rest, an infected creature must make a DC 11 Constitution saving throw. On a failed save, the character gains one level of exhaustion. On a successful save, the character's exhaustion level decreases by one level. If a successful saving throw reduces the infected creature's level of exhaustion below 1, the creature recovers from the disease.

### Cackle Fever ("the shrieks")

- Targets: humanoids (some races, such as gnomes, are noted as immune in this example).
- Incubation: symptoms manifest 1d4 hours after infection.
- Symptoms: fever and disorientation; the infected creature gains one level of exhaustion that cannot be removed until the disease is cured.
- Behavioural effect: while in the grips of this disease, victims frequently succumb to fits of mad laughter, which gives the disease its common name and nickname, "the shrieks." These fits are disruptive and can incapacitate the victim as described under mad laughter effects (see Mad Laughter, below).
- Recovery: follow the disease's specified end-of-long-rest saving throw if provided (see the generic saving-throw progression above or the specific DC in the disease's entry).

### Sight Rot

- Transmission: a beast or humanoid that drinks water tainted by sight rot must succeed on a DC 15 Constitution saving throw or become infected.
- Incubation and onset: one day after infection, the creature's vision starts to become blurry.
- Mechanical effects: the infected creature suffers a −1 penalty to attack rolls and to ability checks that rely on sight. The disease is painful and can cause bleeding from the eyes and eventually blind the victim if untreated.
- Special triggers: any event that causes the infected creature great stress—including entering combat, taking damage, experiencing fear, or having a nightmare—may force the creature to make an additional Constitution saving throw (the disease entry specifies the DC and effects for such stress-triggered rolls). On a failed stress-triggered save the disease may inflict immediate harm or incapacitating effects, and the creature can repeat the saving throw at the end of each of its turns to end the effect, as specified by the disease.

## Mad Laughter (example symptom common to some diseases)

- When present, mad laughter is a contagious symptom that imposes mechanical effects.
- Contagion: any humanoid that starts its turn within 10 feet of a creature suffering a fit of mad laughter must succeed on a DC 10 Constitution saving throw or become infected with the disease producing the laughter (if that disease's description mandates infection by proximity).
- Effect on target: a creature afflicted by a fit of mad laughter may be incapacitated and may take psychic damage as specified by the source disease (examples in sample text include taking 5 (1d10) psychic damage on a failed save). The afflicted creature can repeat the saving throw at the end of each of its turns, ending the laughter on a success.

## Interactions

- Magic and healing: many diseases are curable by Lesser Restoration or similar magic; the disease entry states whether a particular disease is curable by such effects. When a disease entry identifies a cure, apply that cure's effects exactly.
- Conditions and other systems: diseases can impose conditions (incapacitated, blinded), exhaustion levels, damage, or penalties to ability checks and attacks. Apply these effects in the same way as other rules for conditions, exhaustion, and damage.
- Adventure design: a disease that affects more than a few party members is primarily a plot device. The rules above provide mechanical support; the DM should decide how the disease features in the story (searching for a cure, stopping an outbreak, dealing with social consequences).

## Design notes for DMs

- Alter save DCs, incubation times, and symptom severity to fit the desired threat level and story impact.
- Preserve discrete mechanical cases (saves, damage values, exhaustion levels, and progression rules) when adapting these diseases to ensure consistent play.
- When implementing outbreak mechanics that change save DCs or cause cumulative effects, track progression clearly and communicate the stakes to players.
