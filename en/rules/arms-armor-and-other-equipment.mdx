# Arms, Armor, and Other Equipment

## Overview

This section explains currency, selling treasure and equipment, the value of gems/jewelry/art objects and trade goods, coin weights and exchange rates, and basic rules for armor proficiency and armor class (AC). These are practical, mechanical rules for handling money and wearable protection in play.

## Mechanics

### Currency and Common Purchases

- The common coinage used in commerce is, from least to most valuable: copper piece (cp), silver piece (sp), electrum piece (ep), gold piece (gp), and platinum piece (pp).
- The gold piece (gp) is the standard unit of value for measuring wealth. Practical examples of what 1 gp buys: a bedroll, 50 feet of good rope, or a goat.
- A skilled (but not exceptional) artisan can earn 1 gp per day.
- A standard coin weighs about one-third of an ounce; 50 coins weigh a pound.

### Selling Treasure and Equipment

- Normally, adventurers sell treasures, equipment, and trinkets when they return to a town or other settlement, provided they can find buyers and interested merchants.
- Weapons, armor, and other equipment that are undamaged fetch about half their cost when sold in a market.
- Weapons and armor used by monsters are rarely in good enough condition to resell.
- Selling magic items is problematic: finding buyers or merchants who will purchase magic items is difficult, and the value of magic is typically far beyond simple coin. Treat magic items as special — they often require specialist buyers and fora rather than ordinary shops.
- Finding someone to buy a potion or a scroll is relatively easy; finding buyers for many other magic items typically requires travel to wealthier towns or nobles, or the intervention of collectors and specialists.

### Gems, Jewelry, Art Objects, and Trade Goods

- Gems, jewelry, and art objects retain their full value in the marketplace and can be traded in for coin or used as currency for other transactions.
- Exceptionally valuable treasures may require finding a buyer in a large town or community first.
- Trade goods (for example: bars of iron, bags of salt, oil, livestock) retain their full value in the market and can be used as currency—especially in regions where coinage is uncommon and barter is prevalent.

### Standard Exchange Rates

The following table shows common conversion rates between coin types. Each entry gives the value of the coin in terms of the column headings.

| Coin | cp | sp | ep | gp | pp |
| --- | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: | ---: |
| Copper (cp) | 1 | 1/10 | 1/50 | 1/100 | 1/1000 |
| Silver (sp) | 10 | 1 | 1/5 | 1/10 | 1/100 |
| Electrum (ep) | 50 | 5 | 1 | 1/2 | 1/20 |
| Gold (gp) | 100 | 10 | 2 | 1 | 1/10 |
| Platinum (pp) | 1,000 | 100 | 20 | 10 | 1 |

Notes:
- 1 gp = 10 sp. 1 sp = 10 cp. 1 ep = 5 sp (or 50 cp). 1 pp = 10 gp (or 100 sp or 1,000 cp).
- Electrum (ep) and platinum (pp) sometimes appear in treasure hoards but are less common in everyday circulation.

### Armor Categories and Shields

- Armor in the game is organized into three categories: light armor, medium armor, and heavy armor. Many warriors also use shields.
- The armor table (not reproduced here) collects the most commonly available types of armor and lists their cost, weight, and other properties. Armor types range from leather to chain mail to costly plate armor, with other variations between.

### Armor Proficiency

- Anyone can put on a suit of armor or strap a shield to an arm. Only those with proficiency in the armor's use know how to wear it effectively.
- Proficiency in armor is granted by your class (or other features). If you wear armor that you lack proficiency with, you suffer the following mechanical penalties:
  - You have disadvantage on any ability check, saving throw, or attack roll that involves Strength or Dexterity.
  - You cannot cast spells while wearing armor you are not proficient with.

### Armor Class (AC)

- Armor (and shields) determine your base Armor Class. The exact base AC value depends on the specific armor or shield being worn (see the game's armor table for precise AC and property values).

## Interactions

- Currency and treasure interaction: Gems, jewelry, art objects, and trade goods are generally easier to convert to coin than specialized or damaged equipment. Magic items are treated specially and normally require special buyers.
- Weight and encumbrance: Coin weight can add up—50 coins weigh a pound—so large sums carried as coinage contribute to carried weight and encumbrance calculations.
- Armor proficiency affects many systems: wearing armor without proficiency imposes disadvantage on Strength- and Dexterity-related checks, saving throws, and attack rolls, and prevents spellcasting. This interacts with class abilities, spellcasting, skill checks, and combat tactics.
- Marketplace scale: For exceptionally valuable items, the GM may require travel to a large town or community to find a buyer, or payment in forms other than individual coins (gold bars, letters of credit, or valuable goods).
