# Saving Throws

## Overview

Saving throws (saves) represent a creature's attempt to resist a harmful effect such as a spell, trap, poison, disease, or similar threat. This section describes when a saving throw happens, how to make one, who is proficient, and how the result is applied by the effect that called for the save.

## Mechanics

### When a save is made

- A saving throw is made when a character or creature is forced to make one because it is at risk of harm from an effect. The GM or the effect specifies when a save is required.

### How to make a saving throw

- Roll a d20 and add the appropriate ability modifier for the type of save (for example, add your Dexterity modifier for a Dexterity saving throw).
- A saving throw can be modified by situational bonuses or penalties as determined by the GM.
- Advantage and disadvantage apply to saving throws in the same way they apply to attack rolls and ability checks.

### Proficiency and creatures

- Each class grants proficiency in at least two saving throws. For example, the wizard is proficient in Intelligence saving throws.
- Proficiency in a saving throw lets a character add his or her proficiency bonus to saves made using that ability score.
- Some monsters have saving throw proficiencies as well; those proficiencies are listed on their stat blocks.

### Determining the save DC

- The Difficulty Class (DC) for a saving throw is determined by the effect that causes it.
- For spells, the DC is determined by the caster's spellcasting ability and proficiency bonus.
  - (Source text: the DC for a saving throw allowed by a spell is determined by the caster’s spellcasting ability and proficiency bonus.)

### Result of a save

- The effect that called for the saving throw specifies the result of a successful or failed save.
- Usually, a successful save means that the creature suffers no harm, or reduced harm, from the effect.

## Related: Charisma checks & spellcasting ability

### Other Charisma checks

Some tasks use Charisma checks rather than saving throws (social tasks and influence). Examples of tasks the GM might ask for a Charisma check include:

- Convincing a chamberlain to let your party see the king.
- Negotiating peace between warring tribes.
- Inspiring a crowd of townsfolk.
- Finding the best person to talk to for news, rumors, and gossip.
- Blending into a crowd to get the sense of key topics of conversation.

### Spellcasting ability

- Bards, paladins, sorcerers, and warlocks use Charisma as their spellcasting ability, which helps determine the saving throw DCs of spells they cast.

## Interactions

- Effects that impose bonuses or penalties, grant advantage or disadvantage, or change proficiency interact with saving throws as described above and as specified by the effect.
- Always consult the specific effect or spell text for exact consequences of a successful or failed save; that text takes precedence for the result.
