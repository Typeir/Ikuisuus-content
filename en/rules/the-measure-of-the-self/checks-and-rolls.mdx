# Checks and Rolls

This page covers the core resolution mechanic: how to make ability checks, how difficulty is set, and how proficiency, cooperation, and saving throws plug into that structure. For Damocles-specific roll variants—advantage, inspiration, Raw Rolls, Confirmed Rolls, and Deliberate Rolls—see [Rolling and Special Rolls](/library/rules/the-measure-of-the-self/rolling-and-special-rolls).

For a complete list of the six ability scores and their associated skills, see [Abilities and Skills](/library/rules/the-measure-of-the-self/abilities-and-skills).

---

## Making an Ability Check

When the outcome of an action is uncertain, the GM calls for an **ability check**:

1. Roll a **d20**.
2. Add the **relevant ability modifier**.
3. Add your **proficiency bonus** if you are proficient in the relevant skill (or if the check itself is one you have proficiency in).
4. Apply any other applicable bonuses or penalties.
5. Compare the total to the **Difficulty Class (DC)**.

If the total **equals or exceeds** the DC, the check succeeds. Otherwise, it fails—meaning no progress, or progress combined with a setback the GM determines.

---

## Difficulty Classes

| Difficulty        |  DC |
| ----------------- | --: |
| Very easy         |   5 |
| Easy              |  10 |
| Medium            |  15 |
| Hard              |  20 |
| Very hard         |  25 |
| Nearly impossible |  30 |

The GM sets the DC before the roll and may adjust it—or grant advantage or impose disadvantage—based on circumstances.

## Proficiency Bonus

Proficiency bonus is a single flat number determined by character level (or reflected in a monster's stat block). It applies to attack rolls, saving throws, and ability checks when proficiency is relevant.

**Rules for applying proficiency:**

- It cannot be added to the same roll more than once, regardless of how many features reference it.
- When a feature says to multiply or divide your proficiency bonus, apply that multiplication once. Do not stack multiple multiplicative effects on the same roll.
- Multiplying a proficiency bonus of 0 still yields 0. A feature that doubles your proficiency for a skill you are not proficient in grants no benefit.

## Contests

When two creatures directly oppose each other—each trying to succeed at something that prevents the other—the GM calls for a **contest**:

1. Both participants make the relevant ability check.
2. The participant with the higher total wins.
3. On a **tie**, the situation remains unchanged; neither side advances.

Contests use the same structure as standard checks, including proficiency and any situational modifiers.

## Working Together

### Helping

When another character assists with a task, the character attempting the check may make it with **advantage**—provided:
- The helper could attempt the task alone.
- The task is one that benefits from additional effort.

In combat, granting help requires the **Help action**.

### Group Checks

When an entire group faces the same challenge, the GM may call for a **group ability check**: every member of the group rolls. If at least **half the group succeeds**, the group as a whole succeeds. Otherwise, the group fails.

Group checks appear when success depends on the whole party keeping pace—navigating a hazardous stretch of terrain, moving quietly through a guarded area, or resisting a collective threat.

## Saving Throws

A **saving throw** represents a creature's instinctive resistance to a forced effect—a spell, trap, poison, or similar threat.

**How to make a saving throw:**
1. Roll a d20.
2. Add the appropriate ability modifier for the type of save (e.g., Dexterity for a Dexterity saving throw).
3. Add your proficiency bonus if you are proficient in that saving throw.
4. Compare the total to the DC set by the effect.

Each class grants proficiency in at least two saving throws. Monster stat blocks list any saving throw proficiencies the creature has.

The DC for a saving throw caused by a spell is set by the caster's spellcasting ability modifier and proficiency bonus. Other effects specify their own DCs.

The effect that called for the save defines what happens on a success or failure—usually, a successful save means no harm or reduced harm.

---

## Average Dice Values

When a feature calls for rolling dice to restore hit points or calculate a similar effect, the rules may permit using a fixed average value instead. Apply the listed average for each die in place of rolling.

_Example: restoring 2d6 hit points becomes a flat 7 instead of rolling._

Use average values consistently within a given feature or ruling to avoid altering expected balance unintentionally.
