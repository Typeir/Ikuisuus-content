# Ability Scores and Modifiers

## Overview

This rule explains the six ability scores that define a creature's physical and mental qualities and how to derive ability modifiers from those scores. It also defines how advantage and disadvantage affect d20 rolls and how those interact with rerolls and special traits.

## Mechanics

Each creature has six ability scores. These scores represent broad capabilities and are used to modify the three main roll types in the game: ability checks, saving throws, and attack rolls.

- Strength — measuring physical power.
- Dexterity — measuring agility.
- Constitution — measuring endurance.
- Intelligence — measuring reasoning and memory.
- Wisdom — measuring perception and insight.
- Charisma — measuring force of personality.

An ability score is a number that defines the magnitude of an ability. A score of 10 or 11 is the normal human average. A score of 18 is the highest score a typical adventurer usually reaches; player characters can have scores as high as 20, and monsters or divine beings can have scores as high as 30.

### Ability modifier

Every ability score has an associated ability modifier. To determine an ability modifier without consulting the table, subtract 10 from the ability score and then divide the result by 2, rounding down.

Modifier = floor((Score − 10) / 2)

The ability modifier is applied to nearly every attack roll, ability check, and saving throw that uses that ability. Ability modifiers appear in play more often than the raw scores themselves.

### Score → Modifier table

| Ability Score | Modifier |
|---------------:|:--------:|

| 2–3            | −4       |
| 4–5            | −3       |
| 6–7            | −2       |
| 8–9            | −1       |
| 10–11          | +0       |
| 12–13          | +1       |
| 14–15          | +2       |
| 16–17          | +3       |
| 18–19          | +4       |
| 20–21          | +5       |
| 22–23          | +6       |
| 24–25          | +7       |
| 26–27          | +8       |
| 28–29          | +9       |
| 30             | +10      |

Modifiers therefore range from −5 (for a score of 1) to +10 (for a score of 30).

### Using ability modifiers

- When making an ability check, add the appropriate ability modifier to the d20 roll, along with any proficiency bonus if applicable, and compare the total to the target number.
- When making a saving throw, add the appropriate ability modifier (and proficiency bonus if the saving throw is one you are proficient in) to the d20 roll and compare to the DC.
- When making an attack roll, add the relevant ability modifier (and proficiency bonus if applicable) to the d20 roll to determine whether you hit.

## Advantage and Disadvantage

Sometimes a special ability, feature, or spell grants advantage or imposes disadvantage on a d20 roll.

- If you have advantage on a roll, roll a second d20 and use the higher of the two rolls.
- If you have disadvantage on a roll, roll a second d20 and use the lower of the two rolls.

You do not roll more than one additional d20. For example, if you have disadvantage and roll a 17 and a 5, you use the 5. If you instead have advantage and roll those numbers, you use the 17.

If multiple circumstances affect a roll and some grant advantage while others impose disadvantage, they cancel each other out: you have neither advantage nor disadvantage, and you roll one d20. This is true even if multiple circumstances grant advantage and only one grants disadvantage (or vice versa); when a roll has both advantage and disadvantage from different sources, you have neither.

When you have advantage or disadvantage and something in the game (such as the halfling's Lucky trait) lets you reroll the d20, you can reroll only one of the dice. You choose which die to reroll. For example, a halfling with advantage rolls a 1 and a 13; the halfling could use Lucky to reroll the 1.

Inspiration can also grant advantage on an ability check. Special abilities, spells, and conditions may grant advantage or impose disadvantage as described in their text.

## Interactions

- Lucky, Inspiration, and similar effects that let you reroll the d20 interact with advantage/disadvantage as above: you may reroll only one d20 when advantage or disadvantage is in effect, and you choose which one to reroll.
- Multiple sources of advantage do not stack to grant additional dice; you never roll more than one extra d20 for advantage or disadvantage.
- If a rule, spell, or trait modifies how advantage or disadvantage works for a specific case, that feature's text overrides the general rule above for that case.
- Ability modifiers apply to attack rolls, checks, and saves; other modifiers (proficiency, situational bonuses, cover, magic, etc.) combine with ability modifiers according to their own rules.
