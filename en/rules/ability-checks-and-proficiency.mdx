# Ability Checks and Proficiency

## Overview

This section describes ability checks, the use of the proficiency bonus with those checks, contests between opposed efforts, and the Typical Difficulty Classes (DCs) commonly used as targets. The GM decides when ability checks are required and may adjust difficulty or grant advantage or disadvantage based on circumstances.

## Typical Difficulty Classes

Common DCs that GMs use as a guideline:

| Difficulty | DC |
|---|---:|
| Very easy | 5 |
| Easy | 10 |
| Medium | 15 |
| Hard | 20 |
| Very hard | 25 |
| Nearly impossible | 30 |

The GM can decide that circumstances influence a task's Typical Difficulty Class and, as a result, grant advantage or impose disadvantage on the roll.

## Making an Ability Check

To make an ability check, roll a d20 and add the relevant ability modifier. Apply any applicable bonuses and penalties, then compare the total to the DC for the task. If the total equals or exceeds the DC, the ability check is a success and the creature overcomes the challenge. Otherwise, it is a failure; failure means the character or monster makes no progress toward the objective or makes progress combined with a setback determined by the GM.

- Roll: d20 + relevant ability modifier + applicable bonuses/penalties.
- Success: total ≥ DC → the action succeeds or the objective is achieved.
- Failure: total < DC → no progress or a setback as determined by the GM.

## Proficiency Bonus

Characters have a proficiency bonus determined by their level; monsters also have this bonus incorporated in their stat blocks. The proficiency bonus is used with ability checks, saving throws, and attack rolls when a creature is proficient in the relevant skill, saving throw, or weapon.

Important rules about applying proficiency:

- Your proficiency bonus can’t be added to a single die roll or other number more than once. For example, if two different rules say you can add your proficiency bonus to a Wisdom saving throw, you add it only once to that roll.
- If a feature or effect allows you to multiply or divide your proficiency bonus, multiply or divide the proficiency bonus only once for that check or roll. You do not add the proficiency bonus multiple times and then multiply.
- If your proficiency bonus is 0 for a check (for example, you are not proficient in that skill), multiplying it yields 0. For instance, if you lack proficiency in the History skill, a feature that doubles your proficiency bonus for Intelligence (History) checks grants no benefit.

Example: A rogue's Expertise feature doubles the proficiency bonus for certain ability checks. That doubling applies only to the proficiency bonus and is applied a single time for the relevant check.

## Multiplying or Dividing Proficiency

When a feature or effect specifies that you multiply or divide your proficiency bonus (for example, doubling it), do the following:

- Determine the base proficiency bonus for the creature (from level or stat block).
- Apply any single multiplicative or divisive modifier once to that bonus.
- Add the resulting value to the check, save, or roll if the creature is otherwise allowed to apply proficiency.

Do not apply multiple multiplicative effects to the same proficiency bonus for the same roll by stacking them; only one multiplication or division applies.

## Contests (Opposed Ability Checks)

Sometimes one character’s or monster’s efforts are directly opposed to another’s. When two creatures attempt opposing actions (for example, one tries to snatch up a magic ring that has fallen to the floor while the other tries to keep hold of it), the GM runs a contest.

- Both participants make ability checks appropriate to their efforts.
- Each participant applies all appropriate bonuses and penalties to their check.
- Compare the two totals. The participant with the higher check total wins the contest.
- If the contest results in a tie, the situation remains as it was before the contest; neither side succeeds. In some cases the GM may rule that one contestant wins by default depending on circumstances.

Contests resolve whether one participant succeeds at the action or prevents the other from succeeding.

## Skills

Each ability covers a broad range of capabilities, including skills that a character or monster can be proficient in. A skill represents a specific aspect of an ability. When a character is proficient in a skill, add the proficiency bonus (after any allowed multiplication/division) to the ability check for that skill.

## Interactions and Special Cases

- Apply bonuses and penalties in the order appropriate for the situation; the final total is what is compared to the DC or to an opponent's contest total.
- The GM adjudicates ambiguous situations and may require a special form of ability check (such as a contest) when two creatures' efforts oppose each other directly.
- The proficiency bonus is a single numeric bonus; features that reference it interact according to the rules above (cannot be added more than once, multiplicative effects apply once, multiplying 0 yields 0).

## Examples from Play

- If two different rules allow adding your proficiency bonus to the same roll, add it only once.
- If a feature doubles your proficiency bonus for a specific check and you are not proficient in that check, doubling 0 still yields 0; you gain no benefit from the feature for that check.
- When a creature’s stat block includes a proficiency bonus, that value is used for checks and saves as appropriate for that creature.

## Summary

Ability checks measure a creature’s talent and training for a task. Use the d20 + ability modifier (plus proficiency when applicable), compare to a DC or an opponent’s contest total, and apply the rules for proficiency application and contests described above.
