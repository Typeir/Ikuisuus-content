# The Eulogy of Damocles

Damocles remains an obituary writ in void. It is not dying, nor fallen, not even waiting to be reclaimed. It is gone.
The universe of Damocles, the tapestry, the Canvas, no longer exists; What you hold — these rules, these pages, these names — is an eulogy; A testament spoken over something already gone.

---

## There Is No Ruin to Visit

This is not a post-apocalyptic setting. An apocalypse implies something survived. Something can be rebuilt. Someone can walk through the wreckage and pick up the pieces. Damocles offers no such comfort.

The Canvas does not dream. The Black Cradle does not spin. The Clone Worlds do not grind against each other in their eternal mimicry. The Everdark does not burn. None of this is happening. It ended. The tale is all that remains.

When you play Damocles, you are not exploring a world. You are reconstructing one. Every session is an act of spoken archaeology — the piecing together of something that was, from fragments of memory, from half-remembered songs, from the names of dead gods and the echoes of civilizations whose languages have been eaten by time.

---

## The Tale Is the Only Artefact

Archaeologists do not visit the past. They sift through what the past left behind and construct a narrative from the evidence. In Damocles, the evidence is the tale itself. The rules are a grammar of remembrance. The dice are the gaps in the record where memory fails.

But none of this is cold. None of this is clinical. The tale of Damocles is vile and beautiful in equal measure. It contains the Red Rot and the Pieni. It contains the Everdark and the Binturian Hinterlands. It contains cruelty that beggars description and kindness so small and so stubborn that it survives three apocalypses in the form of a song a northern shepherd still sings.
The Archaeologist does not merely catalogue, the feel, to tell the tale of Damocles is to look at the evidence and understand why it matters and not let it go, not even in Oblivion.

---

## To Devote oneself to the Doomed

Nothing can be saved in any permanent sense — the Canvas itself has stopped. And yet.

The point of Damocles is not to catalogue the dead with detached precision. It is to look at the uncaring, unstoppable end of all things and say: "I will save this village. Because it matters to me."

The village does not survive into the present. Nothing does. But in the tale, in the retelling, in the reconstruction — That small Binturian village, ever blanketed in snow, still stands. The Red Rot is fought, the Chimera is slain. Not because these acts change the final outcome, but because those involved in it chose to care, despite the uncaring machinery of existence itself.

This is not despair, it is quite the opposite.
Despair says: nothing can be saved, therefore nothing is worth doing.
Damocles says: nothing can be saved, therefore every act of care is an act of defiance.

That is the fun of Damocles. That is the point. To live, for a few hours at a table with friends, inside the tale of a dead universe — and to find, in its ruins, something worth remembering.
To look at the uncaring, unstoppable future end of all things and say, with full knowledge that it will not last: "this mattered. This was beautiful. I was here."
