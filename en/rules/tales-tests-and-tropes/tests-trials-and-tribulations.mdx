# Tests, Trials, and Tribulations

The dice are not the game. They are the adjudicator of uncertainty. Knowing when to invoke them, and at what threshold, is the craft that distinguishes a Game Master from someone reading rules aloud.

---

## When to Roll

The dice enter the conversation only when three conditions are met simultaneously:

1. **The outcome is uncertain.** A character with two working arms and a functional doorknob does not roll to open a door. A character attempting to pick a lock in total darkness while guards patrol ten feet away does.
2. **The outcome is meaningful.** A rope knot tied while hanging from a cliff face warrants a roll. Bootlaces do not.
3. **Failure produces consequence.** A failed roll that yields only silence — "nothing happens" — should not have been rolled. A failed roll produces a snapped lockpick, an alerted guard, a frayed rope. Silence is not an outcome.

### The Automatic Pass

Characters sufficiently skilled in a domain do not roll for routine applications of that skill. A Wizard with +12 to Arcana need not identify a basic spell circle. A Strider with +15 to Survival need not roll to orient themselves in a temperate forest. The dice resolve tension. They do not exist to humiliate competence.

When a character's modifier exceeds the DC by 10 or more — or when the task is simply beneath their demonstrated capability — narrate success without touching the dice.

## Tier-Appropriate Difficulty

### Standard DCs

| Difficulty        | DC  | Typical At                                                             |
| ----------------- | --- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Trivial           | 5   | Tasks a child could accomplish. No competent adventurer should roll.   |
| Easy              | 10  | Routine for someone with passing familiarity.                          |
| Straightforward   | 15  | Requires a baseline of training. A professional's daily work.          |
| Moderate          | 20  | Challenging for a competent character. Requires real skill.            |
| Tough             | 25  | Demands genuine expertise. Even specialists pause.                     |
| Hard              | 30  | A specialist's benchmark. Failure is the expected outcome for most.    |
| Arduous           | 35  | Pushes the limits of dedicated builds. Requires luck and preparation.  |
| Very Hard         | 40  | The upper reach of mortal specialists. Requires dedication and luck.   |
| Nearly Impossible | 50  | Beyond what even legends achieve casually. Requires peak optimization. |
| Legendary         | 60  | A feat that will be remembered. The threshold of myth.                 |

### The World Does Not Level With the Party

A heavy iron door that required a DC 15 Strength check at level 1 remains a DC 15 door at level 15. The door has not changed. The character has.

This is not a flaw. It is the point of progression. When the Warrior who once burned a full round forcing open a portcullis can now lift it with one hand, that is the system acknowledging their growth. The world provides harder doors — tombsteel barriers sealed with Eternity-era warding matrices, requiring simultaneous Arcana and Athletics checks at DC 35 — but the old doors stay old. Challenges escalate because locations escalate, not because reality inflates to match the party's numbers.

### When to Scale

Scale challenges when the party seeks greater danger. Do not scale challenges when the party returns to familiar ground. Thornwood bone-wasps are CR 1/4 at level 1 and remain CR 1/4 at level 15. The party will destroy them. That is the consequence of progression. There are always greater threats in darker places.

## Reading Monster Statistics for DC Guidance

Monster stat blocks are the most honest source of DC calibration in the system.

### Save DCs by Challenge Rating

Damocles uses unbounded accuracy — optimized characters routinely produce results in the 40s and 50s by mid-to-high levels. The bestiary reflects this.

| Challenge Rating | Typical Save DC | Implied Threat Level                   |
| ---------------- | --------------- | -------------------------------------- |
| 1–4              | 10–15           | Local danger. A competent guard.       |
| 5–8              | 14–18           | Regional threat. A trained killer.     |
| 9–12             | 17–22           | Widespread peril. A master of a craft. |
| 13–16            | 20–25           | Kingdom-level danger. A legend.        |
| 17–20            | 23–28           | Continental threat. A demigod.         |
| 21+              | 26–35+          | World-scale peril. Forces beyond life. |

When designing a hazard, trap, social encounter, or environmental effect, consult the nearest monster of appropriate CR. If the party is level 6 and you are designing a trap for a mid-tier ruin, reference CR 5–8 monsters and note their save DCs. Your trap's DC should fall within that range. This grounds ad-hoc DCs in the same mathematical framework as the rest of the system.

### Ability Checks vs. Saving Throws

Monsters also provide benchmarks for ability check thresholds. A creature with Perception +8 notices most hidden characters without magical assistance. A creature with Athletics +12 performs feats of strength that would kill a lesser being. Use these numbers to calibrate the boundary between competence and exception at each tier.

## The Failing Forward Doctrine

Failure must produce motion. The story cannot stall on a missed roll.

A failed lockpick attempt does not end with "the lock remains locked." It ends with footsteps approaching — the guard is coming. The original goal remains achievable, but the cost has risen.

A failed persuasion check does not end with "the NPC is unmoved." It ends with the NPC revealing information unintentionally — "I would never betray the Duke" tells the party the Duke is involved. The NPC has not complied, but the party has learned.

A failed Arcana check to identify a ritual does not end with ignorance. It ends with partial recognition — black salt. Black salt binds souls. You do not know what this ritual does, but you know what it costs. That is enough to act on.
