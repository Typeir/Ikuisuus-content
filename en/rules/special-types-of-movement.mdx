# Special Types of Movement

## Overview

This section governs movement in terrains and situations that are more complex than ordinary walking: difficult terrain, climbing, swimming, crawling, and jumping. It defines how movement costs change, when ability checks are required, and how jumps interact with reach and obstacles.

## Mechanics

- Difficult Terrain

  - Difficult terrain halves your speed. Moving 1 foot in difficult terrain costs 2 feet of movement.
  - As a result, you cover only half the normal distance in a minute, an hour, or a day when all movement is through difficult terrain.
  - Examples include dense forests, deep swamps, rubble-filled ruins, steep mountains, and ice-covered ground. The GM determines whether specific terrain counts as difficult terrain.

- Movement Costs While Climbing, Swimming, or Crawling

  - While climbing or swimming, each foot of movement costs 1 extra foot (that is, 2 feet of movement per foot moved) unless a creature has a climbing or swimming speed.
  - In difficult terrain, climbing or swimming costs an additional foot per foot (that is, 2 extra feet), so movement through difficult terrain while climbing or swimming is particularly costly.
  - At the GM's option, climbing a slippery vertical surface or one with few handholds requires a successful Strength (Athletics) check. Similarly, gaining any distance in rough water might require a successful Strength (Athletics) check.

- Jumping

  - General
    - Your Strength determines how far you can jump. Each foot you clear on a jump costs a foot of movement.
    - You can extend your arms half your height above yourself during a jump. Thus, the distance you can reach above yourself while jumping equals the height of the jump plus 1½ times your height (the jump height plus the extended reach).

  - Long Jump
    1. If you move at least 10 feet on foot immediately before the jump, you cover a number of feet up to your Strength score.
    2. When you make a standing long jump (no 10-foot run-up), you leap only half that distance.
    3. Each foot you clear on the long jump costs a foot of movement.
    4. At the GM's option, you must succeed on a DC 10 Strength (Athletics) check to clear a low obstacle no taller than a quarter of the jump's distance (for example, a hedge or low wall). If you fail that check, you hit the obstacle.

  - High Jump
    1. If you move at least 10 feet on foot immediately before the jump, you leap into the air a number of feet equal to 3 + your Strength modifier.
    2. When you make a standing high jump, you can leap only half that distance.
    3. Each foot you clear on the high jump costs a foot of movement.

  - Landing
    - When you land in difficult terrain after a jump, you must succeed on a DC 10 Dexterity (Acrobatics) check to land on your feet. If you fail that check, you land prone.

## Interactions

- Movement and Travel Pace
  - The travel speeds given in a travel pace table (when used) assume relatively simple terrain such as roads and open plains. Travel pace and overland movement should be adjusted when terrain is difficult or when frequent climbing, swimming, or crawling is required.

- Ability Checks and GM Adjudication
  - The GM may require Strength (Athletics) checks or other ability checks for more hazardous climbing, for moving through very rough water, or for unusual jumping conditions. The DCs presented above (for clearing low obstacles and for landing in difficult terrain) are examples; the GM may set different DCs based on conditions.

- Speeds that Grant Special Movement
  - Creatures with a climbing speed or a swimming speed do not pay the extra movement cost for climbing or swimming described above. Such speeds supersede the default extra-cost rules for those movement modes.

- Difficult Terrain Stacking
  - Apply difficult-terrain movement costs to the base movement mode. For example, if you are climbing in difficult terrain, each foot of movement may cost extra feet according to both climbing and difficult-terrain rules, as described above.

- Obstacles and Reach
  - Use the jump's height plus extended reach (arms extended half your height) to determine whether you can reach ledges, grab overhead objects, or clear obstacles. If an obstacle remains after applying reach, the GM may require checks or treat the jump as failing according to the obstacle rules above.
