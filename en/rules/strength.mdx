# Strength

## Overview

Strength represents bodily power, athletic training, and the raw force a creature can exert. This section explains Strength checks, how characters work together on physically demanding tasks, how Strength modifies attack rolls and damage for certain weapons, and how carrying capacity is calculated.

## Mechanics

### Strength Checks

- A Strength check models any attempt to lift, push, pull, break something, force your body through a space, or otherwise apply brute force. The Athletics skill reflects aptitude in such effort; a Strength (Athletics) check covers climbing, jumping, and swimming.
- When multiple characters team up on a task, the character with the highest relevant ability modifier (the leader) may make the ability check with advantage to reflect help provided by others, if the GM determines that working together would be productive.
- A character can provide help only when the task is one that the helper could attempt alone. For example, opening a lock requires proficiency with thieves' tools, so a character who lacks that proficiency can't help another character with that task.

### Working Together

- In many difficult situations, two or more characters can team up. The GM decides when help applies and whether the help grants advantage by reflecting the assistance of others.
- In combat, providing help to another character's attack or other tasks requires the Help action and follows the rules for that action.

### Group Checks

- When several characters attempt to accomplish something as a group, the GM might call for a group ability check. In a group check, everyone in the group makes the ability check.
- If at least half the group succeeds, the whole group succeeds. Otherwise, the group fails.
- Group checks are most useful when success requires broad cooperation or shared skill; they do not appear often.
- Example: When adventurers are navigating a swamp, the GM might call for a group Wisdom (Survival) check to see if they avoid quicksand, sinkholes, and other hazards. If at least half succeed, the successful characters can guide their companions out of danger; otherwise the group stumbles into a hazard.

### Typical Strength Check Situations

The GM might call for a Strength check in cases such as:

- You attempt to climb a sheer or slippery cliff, avoid hazards while scaling a wall, or cling to a surface while something is trying to knock you off.
- You try to jump an unusually long distance or pull off a stunt midjump.
- You struggle to swim or stay afloat in treacherous currents, storm-tossed waves, or areas of thick seaweed.
- Another creature tries to push or pull you underwater or otherwise interfere with your swimming.

Other common Strength check tasks include:

- Forcing open a stuck, locked, or barred door
- Breaking free of bonds
- Pushing through a tunnel that is too small
- Hanging on to a wagon while being dragged behind it
- Tipping over a statue
- Keeping a boulder from rolling

### Attack Rolls and Damage

- You add your Strength modifier to your attack rolls and to the damage roll when you make an attack with a melee weapon such as a mace, battleaxe, or javelin. Some melee weapons can be thrown to make a ranged attack; you still add your Strength modifier to the attack and damage rolls for those attacks.

### Lifting and Carrying

- Your Strength score determines the amount of weight you can bear. Carrying capacity is calculated as your Strength score multiplied by 15. The result is the weight in pounds that you can carry, which is a high enough number that it seldom comes up in play.

### Using Each Ability

- Every task a character or monster might attempt is covered by one of the six ability scores. This section explains how Strength (and its associated checks and skills) applies to the physical tasks characters encounter in the game.

## Interactions

- Strength checks interact with skills (especially Athletics), the Help action, and group-check rules. The GM adjudicates whether assistance, tools, or special circumstances change the DC, grant advantage, or allow a group check.
- Proficiencies and required tools (for example, thieves' tools for picking locks) can prevent a character from meaningfully helping another character with a task.
