# Traps

## Overview

Traps are hazards—mechanical or magical—placed to injure, impede, or surprise creatures. A trap can be a simple pit or a complex magical ward; it determines how it is triggered, what it does when triggered, and what checks are required to notice, disable, or bypass it. Traps often rely on a mechanism or on spell effects to function and can be found almost anywhere.

## Traps in Play

When characters encounter a trap, the GM determines how the trap is triggered, what it does, and whether the characters have any opportunity to detect or avoid it. A trap’s description should state the trigger, the effect, and the checks (and DCs) to detect and disable it. Use common sense and the trap’s description to adjudicate interactions rather than attempting to anticipate every possible player action.

- A character’s passive Wisdom (Perception) score can determine whether they notice a trap while passing by.
- If adventurers detect a trap before triggering it, they might be able to disarm it, bypass it, or otherwise avoid its effects.
- Allow a character to discover a trap without a check if an action clearly reveals the trigger (for example, lifting a rug that conceals a pressure plate).
- Clever play and good planning should be rewarded; do not let die rolling completely override clever tactics.

Common mechanical trap examples:

- Pits
- Arrow traps
- Falling blocks
- Water-filled rooms
- Whirling blades or scything blades
- Nets or vines that drop or grasp
- Any hazard that depends on a mechanism to operate

Common trap triggers include stepping on a pressure plate, pulling a tripwire, turning a doorknob, touching an object, or using the wrong key in a lock.

## Triggering a Trap

Most traps are triggered when a creature does something the trap’s creator anticipated. Typical triggers:

- Entering an area
- Stepping on a pressure plate or false floor section
- Pulling a tripwire
- Touching or attempting to open an object the trap protects
- Performing a particular action (placing or removing an item, speaking a word, etc.)

Some magic traps use more complex trigger conditions (for example, a glyph of warding that triggers only if a password is not spoken). The trap’s description should make trigger conditions clear.

Example adjudication: if a chest has two handles set in its sides that, when pulled, fire poisoned needles, inspecting the chest and making a few checks might leave the characters unsure whether it’s trapped. Instead of opening it by hand, the characters might prop a shield in front of the chest and push it open at a distance with an iron rod. In that case the trap can still trigger, but its harmful effect (the hail of needles) might be intercepted by the shield.

## Detecting and Disabling a Trap

A trap’s description specifies which checks (if any) can detect it and which checks disable it, plus the relevant DCs. Key rules and options:

- Passive Perception: A character’s passive Wisdom (Perception) score can reveal a trap without taking an action.
- Active searching: A character actively looking for a trap can attempt a Wisdom (Perception) check against the trap’s DC.
- Investigation: A character can attempt an Intelligence (Investigation) check to deduce how a mechanism works or what needs to be done to disable it.
- Disabling with tools: A successful Dexterity check using thieves’ tools can disable or sabotage many mechanical traps.
- Magic traps and Arcana: Any character can attempt an Intelligence (Arcana) check to detect or disarm a magic trap, in addition to any other checks noted in the trap’s description.

DCs are tied to the trap’s description and are the same no matter which qualifying ability check is used to detect or disable the trap. For magic traps, the trap’s description provides the DC for any relevant ability check.

## Mechanical vs. Magical Traps

Traps are either mechanical or magical in nature:

- Mechanical traps depend on physical mechanisms (gears, pressure plates, tripwires, concealed levers). Examples: pits, falling blocks, arrow traps, whirling blades, nets.

- Magic traps are either magical device traps or spell traps:
  - Magical device traps are devices that initiate spell-like effects when activated.
  - Spell traps are actual spells placed to function as traps (for example, glyph of warding or symbol).

Some magic traps have more complicated trigger conditions than mechanical traps (for example, requiring a password to avoid triggering). Dispel magic can be used on magic traps: when dispel magic is used against a magic trap, use the trap’s description to determine the DC and the effect; dispel magic has a chance to disable most magic traps.

## Common Trap Types

### Pits

**Pit** — A simple hole. Creatures falling in take damage based on depth. A character can spot the pit by noticing disturbed flooring, weight shifts underfoot, or an absence of footprints where others should have passed.

**Spiked Pit** — A pit with sharpened stakes at the bottom. Falling creatures take piercing damage in addition to falling damage.

**Poisoned Pit** — A pit with stakes or surfaces coated in poison. Creatures that take piercing or cutting damage must resist the poison.

### Rolling Boulders and Heavy Objects

**Rolling Boulder** — A heavy stone sphere rolls down a passage in a straight line, crushing anything in its path. Creatures must get out of the way or brace for impact.

### Blades and Sharp Objects

**Scything Blades** — Blades swing or spin through a space. Creatures passing through take slashing damage.

**Falling Blocks or Ceiling Collapse** — Heavy blocks or stonework drops from above. Creatures in the area take blunt-force damage and may be pinned or buried.

### Mechanisms with Poisoned Components

**Poisoned Spike or Needle** — Hidden in a lock, handle, or small opening. Opening or manipulating it carelessly delivers poison. A character can detect this by careful inspection; disarming uses thieves' tools or careful manipulation.

### Traps with Secondary Effects

Some traps combine effects—a pit that floods with water, a blade trap that also releases poison gas, or a passage that collapses and seals behind fleeing creatures. The GM adjudicates both effects in sequence.

### Detection and Disarming

Characters can spot traps by careful observation (Perception) or deduction (Investigation). A character can often prevent a trap from activating by wedging an object under a moving part, by bracing a collapsing structure, or by severing a mechanism before it functions. Using thieves' tools or similar implements allows careful disarming without triggering the trap's effect.

## GM Advice and Interactions

- Always check the trap’s description first. It should state the checks, DCs, triggers, and effects.
- Let players attempt reasonable creative solutions (propping a shield, opening at range, levering lids with rods). If a solution clearly avoids or mitigates the trap’s effect, allow it and apply appropriate checks or consequences.
- Do not allow a single die roll to invalidate clever play; weigh both the roll and the tactic.
- When in doubt about whether an action reveals a trap, favor allowing discovery without a roll if the action would clearly expose the trigger (for example, finding and lifting the rug that conceals a pressure plate).

## Examples of Specific Interactions

- A net hidden among branches might drop on travelers who pass underneath; a character who notices the taut line by passive Perception avoids walking beneath it.
- A vine hanging over a cave entrance might grasp and choke anyone who pushes through; a character could check for such obstacles with Perception or Investigation.
- A chest that fires needles when its handles are pulled: characters inspecting the chest may need Investigation, Perception, or thieves’ tools checks to learn of the trap; opening it from behind a shield might still trigger the trap but protect the party.

## Summary of Mechanical Points

- Traps determine detection (passive Perception or active checks), disablement (thieves’ tools, Arcana, Investigation), and effects.
- The trap description supplies DCs and the qualifying checks.
- Mechanical traps rely on mechanisms; magical traps rely on device or spell effects. Dispel magic can disable many magic traps when an appropriate check succeeds.
- Allow reasonable, clever player actions and use the trap’s description and appropriate checks to resolve them.
