# Travel Pace

## Overview

These rules determine how far a character or monster can move in a minute, an hour, or a day while traveling. When keeping track of the passage of time is important, the GM chooses an appropriate time scale (minutes in a dungeon, hours in the wilderness, days for long journeys) and chooses a travel pace for the group. The travel pace affects distance covered and certain effects such as stealth ability and passive Perception.

## Mechanics

### Travel scales

- Dungeon and similar confined environments: use a minute or smaller scale when precise movement matters (searching, creeping, traps). A single minute is appropriate for actions like creeping down a hallway or checking doors.
- Wilderness and road travel: use an hourly scale for travel and navigation, resting, and random encounters.
- Long journeys: use a daily scale for planning and march durations.

### Travel Pace Table

| Pace   | Feet per minute | Miles per hour | Miles per day | Effect |
|--------|------------------:|---------------:|--------------:|--------|
| Fast   | 400 ft           | 4 miles        | 30 miles      | -5 penalty to passive Wisdom (Perception) |
| Normal | 300 ft           | 3 miles        | 24 miles      | — |
| Slow   | 200 ft           | 2 miles        | 18 miles      | Able to use stealth |

- The table states how far the party can move in a period of time and whether the pace has any effect on passive perception or stealth.
- A fast pace makes characters less perceptive (penalty to passive Wisdom (Perception)). A slow pace lets characters attempt to use Stealth while traveling.

### Choosing a pace

- The GM and the group choose normal, fast, or slow pace depending on context. In a dungeon, movement often happens on a minutes scale and choices about creeping, searching, or moving quickly are frequent.
- In a city or wilderness the hour scale is more appropriate for overland travel and navigation.

### Forced March

- The Travel Pace table assumes characters travel for 8 hours in a day. Characters can push on beyond that limit at the risk of exhaustion.
- For each additional hour of travel beyond 8 hours, each character must make a Constitution saving throw at the end of that hour.
- The DC for this saving throw is 10 + 1 for each hour past 8. (DC = 10 + (hours beyond 8)).
- On a failed saving throw, a character suffers one level of exhaustion (see Appendix for exhaustion rules).

Example: If a character travels for 10 hours (2 hours beyond 8), they must make a Constitution saving throw at the end of the 9th hour (DC 11) and at the end of the 10th hour (DC 12). A failed save at either check causes one level of exhaustion.

### Movement and short-term speeds

- Every character and monster has a speed: the distance in feet the creature can move in 1 round (a round is 6 seconds). That speed assumes short bursts of energetic movement in a life-threatening situation.
- In combat and other fast-paced situations where the game uses rounds, use the round scale (6 seconds) for movement and actions.

### Mounts and galloping

- For short spans of time (up to about an hour), many animals can move much faster than humanoids. A mounted character can ride at a gallop for about an hour, covering roughly twice the usual distance for a fast pace.
- If fresh mounts are available at intervals (commonly every 8 to 10 miles), a group can sustain greater distances by changing mounts; this is rare except in populated or well-served regions.

### Wagons, carriages, and waterborne vessels

- Characters in wagons, carriages, or other land vehicles choose a pace as normal. Characters in waterborne vessels are limited to the vessel's speed and do not suffer penalties for a fast pace or gain benefits from a slow pace.
- Depending on the vessel and crew size, ships and other large craft might be able to travel for up to 24 hours per day.

### Summarizing movement between encounters

- The GM can summarize movement between encounters without calculating exact distances or travel times: e.g., “You travel through the forest and find the dungeon entrance late in the evening of the third day.”
- In a large dungeon or cave network the GM may still summarize travel between rooms; in tight corridors the minute scale and careful pacing may be used instead.

## Interactions

- Pace interacts with perception and stealth: fast travel imposes a penalty to passive Wisdom (Perception); slow travel enables the use of Stealth.
- Forced march interacts with exhaustion rules; each failed Constitution save from overlong travel inflicts one level of exhaustion.
- Mount-based gallops and vehicle travel can change how much distance the party covers in a given time, and availability of fresh mounts or crew affects how long high-speed travel is sustainable.
