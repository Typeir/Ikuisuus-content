# Movement and Position

## Overview

This rule governs how creatures move and position themselves in combat, including splitting movement around actions, interacting with the environment while moving, how different speeds interact, reactions that affect movement, difficult terrain, and the prone condition.

## Mechanics

### Breaking Up Your Move

On your turn, you can move a distance up to your speed. You can break up that movement: you may use some of your speed before and after your action. Example: with a speed of 30 feet you can move 10 feet, take your action, then move 20 feet.

- Your movement can include jumping, climbing, and swimming; these modes can be combined with walking, or constitute your entire move.
- As you move, deduct the distance of each part of your move from your remaining speed until it is used up or you are done moving.

### Other Activity on Your Turn

You can communicate using brief utterances and gestures while taking your turn.

You can interact with one object or feature of the environment for free, during either your move or your action. Examples:

- Opening a door as you stride toward a foe.
- Drawing a weapon as part of the same action you use to attack.

If you want to interact with a second object on the same turn, you need to use your action to do so.

Some magic items and special objects require an action to use, as stated in their descriptions. The GM might require you to use an action for activities that need special care or present an unusual obstacle (for example, opening a stuck door or turning a crank to lower a drawbridge).

### Moving between Attacks

If you take an action that includes more than one weapon attack, you can break up your movement even further by moving between those attacks. Example: a fighter with the Extra Attack feature and a speed of 25 feet could move 10 feet, make an attack, move 15 feet, and then make the second attack.

### Using Different Speeds

If you have more than one speed (for example, walking speed and a flying speed), you can switch back and forth between those speeds during your move. Whenever you switch, subtract the distance you have already moved from the new speed; the result determines how much farther you can move using that speed.

- If the result is 0 or less, you can't use the new speed during the current move.

Example: if you have a walking speed of 30 feet and a flying speed of 60 feet (because a spell like fly was cast on you), you could fly 20 feet, then walk 10 feet, then leap into the air to fly 30 feet more.

### Reactions

Certain special abilities, spells, and situations allow you to take a special action called a reaction. A reaction is an instant response to a trigger, which can occur on your turn or someone else's.

- The opportunity attack is the most common type of reaction.
- When you take a reaction, you can't take another reaction until the start of your next turn.
- If a reaction interrupts another creature's turn, that creature continues its turn right after the reaction.

### Difficult Terrain

Combat often occurs in areas that impede movement. Examples: low furniture, rubble, undergrowth, steep stairs, snow, shallow bogs, boulder-strewn caverns, briar-choked forests, treacherous staircases.

- Every foot of movement in difficult terrain costs 1 extra foot. In other words, moving 1 foot in difficult terrain uses 2 feet of your speed.
- This rule is true even if multiple things in a space count as difficult terrain.
- The space of another creature, whether hostile or not, also counts as difficult terrain for movement through that space.

### Being Prone

Combatants sometimes end up lying on the ground (prone), either because they are knocked down or because they throw themselves down.

- You can drop prone without using any of your speed.
- Standing up from prone costs movement: doing so uses movement equal to half your speed.

## Interactions

- Movement interacts with actions (Extra Attack, Dash, Disengage) and with features that modify or add movement (spells, class features). When features grant extra movement or attacks, apply the above rules (breaking up movement, moving between attacks) as written.
- Difficult terrain and hostile creature spaces increase the movement cost and therefore reduce how far you can move that turn.
- The GM can rule special cases where interacting with objects requires an action or extra time; follow those rulings for that encounter.

## Examples (preserved from source)

- A creature with 30 ft speed and a flying speed of 60 ft could: fly 20 ft, walk 10 ft, then fly 30 ft more.
- A creature with 25 ft speed and Extra Attack could move 10 ft, attack, move 15 ft, then attack again.
