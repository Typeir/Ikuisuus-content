# Damage

## Overview

This page covers everything that happens after an attack hits or an effect triggers: how damage is calculated, how damage types, resistance, and vulnerability interact, what happens when hit points run out, and how healing and temporary hit points work.

---

## Hit Points

Hit points represent a combination of physical and mental durability, the will to live, and luck. A creature's current hit points can be any number from 0 up to its hit point maximum.

- Whenever a creature takes damage, subtract that damage from its hit points.
- The loss of hit points has no effect on a creature's capabilities until the creature drops to 0 hit points.
- Damage can never reduce hit points below 0, nor can it be a negative value.

---

## Damage Rolls

Each weapon, spell, and harmful monster ability specifies the damage it deals. Roll the damage die or dice, add any modifiers, and apply the result to the target.

- When attacking with a weapon, add your ability modifier — the same modifier used for the attack roll — to the damage.
- A spell tells you which dice to roll for damage and whether to add any modifiers.
- Magic weapons, special abilities, and other factors can grant a bonus to damage.
- When an effect damages more than one target simultaneously, roll the damage once and apply the same result to each creature caught in the effect.

---

## Critical Hits

When you score a critical hit, roll **all** of the attack's damage dice twice, then add any relevant modifiers once. Apply the total as normal damage.

- This includes bonus dice from features such as Sneak Attack — all of them are doubled.
- Static modifiers (ability scores, magic bonuses) are added once, not doubled.

---

## Damage Types

Each damage instance has a type that determines what resistances and vulnerabilities apply. If a new effect lacks an obvious type, the GM assigns one.

| Type       | Examples                                                               |
| ---------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Acid       | Black dragon breath, black pudding                                     |
| Bludgeoning | Hammers, falling, constriction                                        |
| Cold       | Ice devil's spear, white dragon breath                                 |
| Fire       | Flames, dragon fire; nonmagical fire is still fire damage              |
| Force      | Magic missile, spiritual weapon — pure magical energy                  |
| Lightning  | Lightning bolt, blue dragon breath                                     |
| Necrotic   | Withering undead attacks, chill touch                                  |
| Piercing   | Spears, bites, arrows                                                  |
| Poison     | Venomous stings, green dragon breath                                   |
| Psychic    | Mind flayer psionic blasts, mental assault                             |
| Radiant    | Divine smites, angel attacks, flame strike                             |
| Slashing   | Swords, axes, claws                                                    |
| Thunder    | Concussive sound, thunderwave                                          |
| True       | Cannot be resisted or avoided. Rare; reserved for exceptional effects. |

## Resistance and Vulnerability

**Resistance** to a damage type halves incoming damage of that type. **Vulnerability** doubles it. Both are applied after all other modifiers to the damage total.

- Apply fixed reductions (magical auras, flat damage reduction features) first, then apply resistance or vulnerability to the resulting total.
- Multiple instances of resistance or vulnerability to the same damage type count as only one instance — they do not stack.
- If an effect deals multiple damage types, apply resistance or vulnerability separately to each type before summing.

### Examples

- A creature with resistance to bludgeoning takes 25 damage; a magical effect reduces all damage by 5 first, so the total is reduced to 20, then halved to **10**.
- A creature vulnerable to fire takes fire damage — double the total after other modifiers.
- Resistance to fire and resistance to all nonmagical damage do not stack: fire damage is halved once.

---

## Healing

Healing restores a creature's hit points up to its hit point maximum. Any healing beyond that maximum is lost.

A creature that has died cannot regain hit points until restored to life by appropriate magic.

---

## Temporary Hit Points

Temporary hit points are a buffer against damage. They are not real hit points — they sit on top of your hit point total and absorb damage first. When a source of damage depletes your temporary hit points, any remaining damage carries over to your actual hit points.

- Temporary hit points cannot exceed the stated amount; if you receive temporary hit points while you already have some, take the higher value rather than adding them together.
- Healing does not restore temporary hit points; they must be granted by a specific feature or effect.
- Unless a feature specifies a duration, temporary hit points last until depleted or until you finish a long rest.

---

## Dropping to 0 Hit Points

When a creature drops to 0 hit points, it either dies outright or falls unconscious, as determined by subsequent rules covering death and dying. Massive damage or effects that kill outright bypass the unconscious state entirely.

---

## Interactions

- Damage types interact with resistances from items, class features, spells, and creature traits. Always determine the damage type first, then apply flat reductions, then resistance or vulnerability.
- The GM assigns a damage type to ambiguous effects — choose the type that best fits the description and intent.
- Critical hits double all damage dice. If Sneak Attack, Divine Smite, or similar add dice to an attack, those dice are doubled on a critical hit.
