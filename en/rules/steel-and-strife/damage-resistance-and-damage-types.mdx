# Damage Resistance and Damage Types

## Overview

This section explains how damage types, resistance, and vulnerability interact with damage rolls, critical hits, and healing. It also lists the common damage types and gives concrete examples of application order when multiple modifiers affect a damage instance.

## Mechanics

- Critical hits: When you score a critical hit, roll all the damage dice associated with the attack (including extra dice from features such as Sneak Attack) twice, then add any relevant modifiers (such as ability modifiers) once.

- Damage resistance and vulnerability:
  - Resistance to a damage type means damage of that type is halved against the creature. Apply resistance after all other modifiers to the damage (see examples below).
  - Vulnerability to a damage type means damage of that type is doubled against the creature. Apply vulnerability after all other modifiers.
  - Multiple instances of resistance or vulnerability that affect the same damage type count as only one instance.

- Order of application: Apply fixed reductions and other modifiers (such as a flat damage reduction from a magical aura) first, then apply resistance or vulnerability to the resulting total for that damage type.

### Examples

- If a creature takes 25 bludgeoning damage and a magical effect reduces all damage by 5, the 25 is reduced to 20 and then halved if the creature has resistance to bludgeoning, so it takes 10 damage.

- If a creature has resistance to nonmagical bludgeoning and also to fire damage, and it is hit by nonmagical fire that deals X damage, reduce that nonmagical fire damage by half (resistance applies once per damage type, not stacked).

- If a creature is vulnerable to a damage type, double the damage of that type after applying other modifiers. For example, vulnerability to fire means fire damage is doubled after other adjustments.

- When determining how many dice to roll for critical hits, include all dice that the effect or feature normally grants (for example, a rogue's Sneak Attack dice are rolled twice on a critical hit).

## Damage Types

The following damage types are commonly used. Each type has examples to help the GM assign a type to new or unusual effects. If a new effect lacks an obvious type, the GM assigns the type.

- Acid: Corrosive sprays and dissolving enzymes (for example, a black dragon's breath or a black pudding dealing acid damage).
- Bludgeoning: Blunt-force attacks such as hammers, falling, constriction, and the like.
- Cold: Frigid blasts and infernal chill (for example, an ice devil's spear or a white dragon's breath).
- Fire: Conjured flames and dragon breath; nonmagical fire is still fire damage and may interact with resistances to nonmagical damage.
- Force: Pure magical energy focused into a damaging form (examples: magic missile, spiritual weapon). Many effects that deal force damage are spells.
- Lightning: Electrical effects (for example, lightning bolt or a blue dragon's breath).
- Necrotic: Damage that withers matter and the soul, often associated with undead or spells such as chill touch.
- Piercing: Puncturing and impaling attacks, including spears and many monsters' bites.
- Poison: Venomous stings and toxic gas (for example, a green dragon's breath).
- Psychic: Mental assaults and psionic effects (for example, a mind flayer's psionic blast).
- Radiant: Searing, spirit-overloading damage from divine or celestial sources (for example, a cleric's flame strike or an angel's smite).
- Slashing: Swords, axes, and monsters' claws.
- Thunder: Concussive bursts of sound such as the thunderwave spell.
- True Damage: Damage that can't be resisted or avoided in any way. True damage is rare and typically reserved for powerful magic or legendary creatures.

## Applying Resistance and Vulnerability

- Resistance and then vulnerability are applied after calculating the damage total (including dice and modifiers) for each damage type. If an effect deals multiple damage types, apply resistance or vulnerability separately to each relevant type before summing the final damage.

- Instances that affect the same damage type count only once. For example, having resistance to fire and resistance to all nonmagical damage does not halve the same fire damage multiple times; it still counts as a single instance of resistance to the fire damage dealt.

## Healing and Hit Points

- Healing restores a creature's hit points up to its hit point maximum. A creature's current hit points cannot exceed its hit point maximum; any healing that would increase current hit points above the maximum instead restores the creature to its maximum and any excess healing is lost.

- A creature that has died can't regain hit points until it is restored to life by powerful magic (for example, the revivify spell or other appropriate effects).

## Dropping to 0 Hit Points

- When a creature drops to 0 hit points, it either dies outright or falls unconscious, as explained in the subsequent sections of the rules. The precise outcome depends on failing death saving throws, massive damage, or effects that kill outright.

## Interactions

- Damage types interact with other rules such as resistances granted by magic items, class features, spells, and creature traits. Always determine the damage type of an effect first, apply fixed modifiers and reductions, and then apply resistance or vulnerability.

- The GM should assign a damage type to ambiguous or new effects. When in doubt, choose the type that best fits the effect's description and the intent of the ability or creature.
