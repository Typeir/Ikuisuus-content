# Actions in Combat

This page lists the standard actions available to creatures in combat. On your turn you take one action: a class action, an action presented here, or an improvised action the GM allows. When an action is described elsewhere, the GM tells you whether it is possible and what roll (if any) is required to determine success.

## Mechanics

### Taking an Action

- On your turn you take one action. This may be an action granted by your class or a special feature, an action listed below, or an improvised action determined by the GM.
- Many monsters have action options in their stat blocks.

### Attack

- The Attack action is the most common action in combat. When you take the Attack action, you make one melee or ranged attack.
- Certain features, such as the Fighter's Extra Attack, allow you to make more than one attack when you take this action. If you make more than one attack, the first attack roll that hits or misses follows the normal rules; features that modify subsequent attacks apply as written.
- See the “Making an Attack” rules for the specifics that govern attack rolls, hit determination, and damage.

### Dodge

- When you take the Dodge action, you focus entirely on avoiding attacks.
- Until the start of your next turn:
  - Any attack roll made against you has disadvantage if you can see the attacker.
  - You make Dexterity saving throws with advantage.
- You lose these benefits if you are incapacitated (as explained in appendix PH\A) or if your speed drops to 0.

### Help

- You can lend your aid to another creature in the completion of a task by taking the Help action.
- When you take the Help action, the creature you aid gains advantage on the next ability check it makes to perform the task you are helping with, provided that it makes that check before the start of your next turn.
- Alternatively, you can use the Help action to aid a friendly creature attacking a creature within 5 feet of you. If you do so, the first attack roll made against that target before your next turn is made with advantage.

### Hide

- When you take the Hide action, you make a Dexterity (Stealth) check in an attempt to hide, following the rules for hiding.
- If you succeed, you gain the benefits described in the “Unseen Attackers and Targets” section until you are discovered or you stop hiding.

### Cast

- Spellcasters (and many monsters) have access to spells and can use them in combat. Each spell has a casting time: it may require an action, a reaction, minutes, or even hours to cast. Because of that, casting a spell is not always an action.
- Most spells have a casting time of 1 action, so a spellcaster often uses his or her action in combat to cast a spell.

### Ready (using your action to Ready)

- Sometimes you want to get the jump on a foe or wait for a particular circumstance before you act. The Ready action lets you act using your reaction before the start of your next turn.
- To use Ready:
  1. First, you decide what perceivable circumstance will trigger your reaction.
  2. Then you choose the action you will take in response to that trigger, or you choose to move up to your speed in response to it. Examples of triggers include “If the cultist steps on the trapdoor, I’ll pull the lever that opens it,” and “If the goblin steps next to me, I move away.”
- When the trigger occurs, you can either take your reaction right after the trigger finishes or ignore the trigger. Remember that you can take only one reaction per round.
- When you ready a spell, you cast it as normal but hold its energy, which you release with your reaction when the trigger occurs. To be readied, a spell must have a casting time of 1 action, and
  the caster holds the spell's energy until the reaction is used or the opportunity passes. (Source text truncated.)

## Movement

- When you take the Movement action, you can move up to your speed. You can break up your movement on your turn, moving before and after your action, but you can't move more than your speed on your turn.

## Steady

**Steady** is a combat state that represents planting your feet and sacrificing mobility for precision and power.

### Declaring Steady

On your turn, you can declare **Steady** when you take your **Movement action**. When you do:

- Your movement speed is **reduced to 5 feet** for the rest of your turn, regardless of your normal speed or any bonuses to it.
- You gain the **Steady** state, which lasts until the end of your turn.

### Steady-Gated Abilities

While in the Steady state, you can use any ability, feature, maneuver, or rule that specifies **Steady** as a requirement. These unlock simultaneously at no additional cost — entering Steady is the only price.

Examples of things that require Steady include:

- [Flanking](/en/library/rules/steel-and-strife/contextual-conflict#flanking) an enemy with an ally
- Certain martial maneuvers granted by Fighting Style chain feats
- Feat features that require a planted stance (e.g. [Swift and Steady](/en/library/character-creation/feats/swift-and-steady), long-range [Sharpshooter](/en/library/character-creation/feats/sharpshooter) precision)

### Design Note

Steady is intentionally punishing for all classes, including those with high movement speeds. The power of a planted stance should be proportional to the cost of giving up that mobility.

### Dash

- When you take the Dash action, you gain extra movement for the current turn. The increase equals your speed after applying any modifiers.
- For example, with a speed of 30 feet, you can move up to 60 feet on your turn if you Dash.
- Any increase or decrease to your speed changes this additional movement by the same amount. If your speed of 30 feet is reduced to 15 feet, for instance, you can move up to 30 feet this turn if you Dash.
- Dash can be used interchangeably with the "Action Degradation" mechanic described in the "Turns and Initiative" rules - degrading your action to gain an extra move action is functionally equivalent to taking the Dash action, and the two can be used together if you have the appropriate actions to sacrifice.

### Disengage

- When you take the Disengage action, your movement doesn't provoke opportunity attacks for the rest of the turn.

### Use

- You can interact with one object or feature of the environment for free, during either your move or your action. For example, you could open a door as you move toward it, or you could draw a weapon as part of the same action you use to attack.
- If you want to interact with a second object, you need to use your action. For example, you could use your action to open a second door, pull a lever, or pick up a dropped axe.
- Certain objects, such as [_Heirlooms_](/en/library/items/heirlooms), often require this action to be used to interact with them.
- [_Trinkets_](/en/library/items/trinkets) require the Use action to interact with them, but it might be possible to take that action as part of your Bonus Action or Reaction if the trinket's description allows it.

### Drop

- You can drop anything you are holding for free, without using an action. This includes weapons, shields, and other items you are holding in your hands, as well as items you are carrying in a backpack or similar container.

### Search

When you take the Search action, you devote your attention to locating something. The GM may call for a Wisdom (Perception) or Intelligence (Investigation) check depending on what you seek.

## Reaction Limits and Notes

- You can take only one reaction per round unless a feature says otherwise. Ready and other reaction uses consume that reaction.
- Certain features may modify the number or type of actions you can take (for example, Extra Attack) — follow those feature descriptions.

## Parlay

A creature may use its **Action** to attempt to **Parlay** with a hostile creature during combat.

The acting creature makes a **Persuasion**, **Intimidation**, or **Deception** check contested by the target’s **Insight**.

- **On a failure**: the attempt has no effect.
- **On a success**: the acting creature gains **advantage on its next attack roll, saving throw or skill competition that's not directly related to the parlay**,
  and if the target’s disposition can change, it shifts one step toward **Neutral** on the behavior chart.

Any harm done to the creature or it's allies by the Parlayer or their allies immediately shifts them away from **Neutral** and renders parlay unusable until the end of the creatures next turn.

## Inspiration

### Gaining Inspiration

- Your GM can award inspiration for a variety of reasons. Typically, GMs award it when a player plays out personality traits, accepts roleplaying drawbacks presented by a flaw or bond, or otherwise portrays the character in a compelling way.
- The exact circumstances and options for earning inspiration are set by the GM; the GM will tell you how you can earn inspiration in the game.
- You either have inspiration or you don't; you can't stockpile multiple "inspirations" for later use.

### Using Inspiration

- If you have inspiration, you can expend it when you make an attack roll, saving throw, or ability check.
- Spending your inspiration gives you advantage on that roll.
- Additionally, if you have inspiration you can reward another player by giving up your inspiration for good roleplaying, clever thinking, or simply doing something exciting in the game. When another player character does something that really contributes to the story in a fun and interesting way, you can give up your inspiration to give that character inspiration.

## Notes for GMs

- Use inspiration as a flexible reward for memorable roleplaying instead of a rigid currency. Define clear, campaign-appropriate triggers for awarding inspiration so players understand what sort of play is rewarded.
- Encourage players to use their inspiration when they have it, rather than hoarding it for later. The point of inspiration is to reward and encourage roleplaying in the moment, not to serve as a long-term resource.

## On the GM's role in action resolution

- The GM resolves improvised actions and determines required rolls when actions are not detailed in the rules.
- Abilities and features that modify attacks, movement, or reactions interact with these standard actions according to their own rules. For example, Extra Attack modifies the Attack action by granting additional attacks; effects that impose disadvantage or grant advantage apply to individual attack rolls as described in their entries.
- Appropriate use of actions in a roleplaying context is a key part of the game, and the GM should encourage players to think creatively about how to use their turn, using inspiration as a reward for interesting and effective play. The GM should also be prepared to adjudicate creative uses of actions that may not be explicitly covered in the rules, using the principles of the game and the spirit of fun and storytelling to guide decisions.
