# Initiative

## Overview

Initiative determines the order of turns during a combat encounter. The GM organizes the chaos of combat into a cycle of rounds and turns; one round represents about 6 seconds of game time. When combat starts, every participant makes a Dexterity check (called an initiative roll) to determine their place in the initiative order. The initiative order remains the same from round to round for the duration of the encounter unless the GM or a game effect changes it.

## The Order of Combat

- At the beginning of a combat encounter, each participant involved rolls initiative (a Dexterity check) to determine when they act during the round.
- The GM ranks the combatants in order from the one with the highest Dexterity check total to the one with the lowest; that ranking is the initiative order in which they act during each round.
- The GM can make one roll for an entire group of identical creatures so each member of that group acts at the same time.
- If a tie occurs between tied GM-controlled creatures, the GM decides the order among them. If a tie occurs between tied player characters, the players decide the order among their tied characters. If a tie is between player characters and monsters, the GM can decide the order or optionally have the tied characters and monsters each roll a d20 to determine who goes first (highest roll goes first).
- Once everyone has taken a turn, the round ends; repeat the initiative order each round until the fighting stops or one side is defeated.

## Combat Step by Step

1. Determine surprise. The GM determines whether anyone involved in the combat encounter is surprised.
2. Establish positions. The GM decides where all the characters and monsters are located (how far away and in what direction). Given the adventurers' stated positions or marching order, the GM figures out where the adversaries are and how far away they stand.
3. Roll initiative. Everyone involved in the combat encounter rolls initiative, determining the order of combatants' turns.
4. Take turns. Each participant in the battle takes a turn in initiative order.
   - On your turn, you can move a distance up to your speed and take one action.
   - You decide whether to move first or take your action first, unless a feature or effect specifies otherwise.
   - Your speed (sometimes called your walking speed) is noted on your character sheet.
5. Begin the next round. When everyone involved in the combat has had a turn, the round ends. Repeat step 4 until the fighting stops.

The most common actions you can take are described in the "Actions in Combat" rules. Many class features and other abilities provide additional options for your action. The "Movement and Position" section contains the rules for your move.

## Your Turn

- On your turn you may move and take an action. You can move a distance up to your speed and take one action. You choose whether to move first or take your action first unless an effect dictates otherwise.
- You can forgo moving, taking an action, or doing anything at all on your turn. If undecided, consider taking the Dodge or Ready action as described in "Actions in Combat."
- If you can't decide what to do on your turn, taking the Dodge or Ready action are valid choices.

## Surprise

- Surprise occurs when one side of the battle does not notice a threat that initiates combat (for example, an ambush). The GM determines who might be surprised.
- If neither side tries to be stealthy, they automatically notice each other. Otherwise, the GM compares the Dexterity (Stealth) checks of anyone hiding with the passive Wisdom (Perception) scores of the creatures on the opposing side.
- Any character or monster that doesn't notice a threat is surprised at the start of the encounter.
- A surprised creature can't move or take an action on its first turn of the combat, and it can't take a reaction until that turn ends.
- A member of a group can be surprised even if other members of the group aren't.

## Bonus Actions

- Various class features, spells, and other abilities allow you to take an additional action on your turn called a bonus action.
- You can take a bonus action only when a special ability, spell, or other feature of the game states that you can do something as a bonus action.
- You otherwise don't have a bonus action to take.
- You can take only one bonus action on your turn, so when you have more than one available you must choose which one to use.
- You choose when to take a bonus action during your turn, unless the bonus action's timing is specified by the feature that grants it.

## Interactions

- Initiative ties: the GM resolves ties among GM-controlled creatures; players resolve ties among their tied characters; the GM may require tied monsters and PCs to roll d20 to break ties.
- Initiative remains fixed from round to round; effects that change turn order must explicitly state how and when they modify initiative.
- Surprise prevents movement, actions, and reactions for the surprised creature on its first turn, which can interact with features that grant reactions or free movement.
- Group initiative (one roll for identical creatures) means area-control tactics and abilities that target the group may affect multiple creatures acting on the same initiative count.

## Notes

- A round represents about 6 seconds in the game world.
- The GM organizes combat into rounds and turns to structure play and simulate simultaneous actions.
