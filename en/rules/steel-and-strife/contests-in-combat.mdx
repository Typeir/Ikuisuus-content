# Contests in Combat

## Overview

This section collects common contest-based combat actions and related fundamentals used to resolve physical contests in combat (for example, grappling and shoving), and the core systems that govern hit points, damage, healing, cover, and damage rolls. These rules describe how to perform the contests, which checks are used, and how their results affect position and hit point totals.

## Mechanics

### Moving a Grappled Creature

- When you move, you can drag or carry a creature you have grappled with you, but your speed is halved while you do so unless the creature is two sizes smaller than you or more.  
- A target with total cover can’t be targeted directly by an attack or a spell, although some spells can reach such a target by including it in an area of effect.  
- A target has total cover if it is completely concealed by an obstacle (for example, a portcullis, an arrow slit, or a thick tree trunk).

### Shoving a Creature

- Using the Attack action, you can make a special melee attack to shove a creature, either to knock it prone or to push it 5 feet away from you.  
- If you can make multiple attacks with the Attack action, the shove replaces one of those attacks.  
- The target must be no more than one size larger than you and must be within your reach.  
- Instead of making an attack roll, you make a Strength (Athletics) check contested by the target’s Strength (Athletics) or Dexterity (Acrobatics) check — the target chooses which ability to use.  
- If you win the contest, you either knock the target prone or push it 5 feet away from you.

### Contests (General guidance)

- Contests pit one creature's ability check against another's. Use the specified ability checks (for shove: Strength (Athletics) vs Strength (Athletics) or Dexterity (Acrobatics)).  
- The attacker or acting creature uses the listed check; the defender chooses the opposed check type when multiple choices are allowed.  
- When a contest succeeds it produces the described mechanical effect (movement, prone, etc.).

### Hit Points

- Hit points represent a combination of a creature’s physical and mental durability, the will to live, and luck.  
- A creature’s current hit points (usually just called its hit points) can be any number from 0 up to the creature’s hit point maximum. That number changes frequently as a creature takes damage or receives healing.  
- Whenever a creature takes damage, subtract that damage from its hit points.  
- The loss of hit points has no effect on a creature’s capabilities until the creature drops to 0 hit points.

### Damage and Healing

- Whenever a creature takes damage, that damage is subtracted from its hit points.  
- Healing increases a creature’s hit points, up to its hit point maximum; damage reduces them.  
- Injury and the risk of death are constant companions for those who explore dangerous places — monitor current hit points closely.

### Cover

- Walls, trees, creatures, and other obstacles can provide cover during combat, making a target more difficult to harm. A target can benefit from cover only when an attack or other effect originates on the opposite side of the cover.  
- There are three degrees of cover. If a target is behind multiple sources of cover, only the most protective degree of cover applies (they do not stack).  

  | Degree of cover | Effect |
  | --- | --- |
  | Half cover | +2 bonus to AC and Dexterity saving throws |
  | Three-quarters cover | +5 bonus to AC and Dexterity saving throws |
  | Total cover | The target can’t be targeted directly by an attack or a spell |

- A target has half cover if an obstacle blocks at least half of its body (examples: a low wall, a large piece of furniture, a narrow tree trunk, or a creature).  
- A target has three-quarters cover if about three-quarters of it is covered by an obstacle.  
- A target has total cover if it is completely concealed by an obstacle and thus can’t be targeted directly by an attack or a spell (though area effects can still affect it).

- Example: If a target is behind a creature that gives half cover and also behind a tree trunk that gives three-quarters cover, the target has three-quarters cover (the most protective degree applies).

### Damage Rolls

- Each weapon, spell, and harmful monster ability specifies the damage it deals. Roll the damage die or dice, add any modifiers, and apply the damage to your target.  
- When attacking with a weapon, add your ability modifier — the same modifier used for the attack roll — to the damage.  
- A spell tells you which dice to roll for damage and whether to add any modifiers.  
- Magic weapons, special abilities, and other factors can grant a bonus to damage.  
- It is possible for modifiers and penalties to result in 0 damage, but damage can never be negative.  
- When an effect deals damage to more than one target at the same time (for example, a fireball or flame strike), roll the damage once for all of the creatures caught in the effect; apply the same result to each relevant creature.

### Critical Hits

- When you score a critical hit, roll extra dice for the attack’s damage against the target.  
- Roll all of the attack’s damage dice twice and then add any relevant modifiers once. Apply the total as normal damage to the target.

## Interactions

- Cover modifies attack rolls and Dexterity saving throws; it does not change how damage is rolled other than making hits less likely.  
- Total cover prevents direct targeting by attacks and many spells; area effects that do not target directly can still affect a creature with total cover if the area includes it.  
- Grappling and dragging a creature reduces movement speed while moving that creature; effects that change a creature’s size or speed can alter grapple and shove legality and outcomes.  
- Contests used for shoves and grapples use the specified ability checks; any feature that alters those checks (advantage, proficiency bonuses, or temporary effects) changes contest outcomes accordingly.
