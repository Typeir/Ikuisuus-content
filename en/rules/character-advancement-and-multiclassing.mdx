# Character Advancement and Multiclassing

## Overview

This document explains how characters advance in level, how experience points translate to levels and proficiency bonus, and the rules for multiclassing. It also covers hit dice and hit point changes when gaining levels and when your Constitution modifier changes.

## Mechanics

### Experience and Character Level

Characters gain experience points (XP) by adventuring and overcoming challenges. When a character reaches the XP total shown in the Character Advancement table, the character advances to the corresponding level and gains any class features that level grants.

### Gaining a Level

- When your character gains a level, the class often grants additional features described in that class's description.
- Each time you gain a level, you gain one additional Hit Die. To increase your hit point maximum when you gain a level, you have two options:
  - Roll the Hit Die for your class, add your Constitution modifier, and add the total to your hit point maximum; or
  - Use the fixed value shown in your class entry (the average result of the die roll, rounded up) instead of rolling.
- When your Constitution modifier increases by 1, your hit point maximum increases by 1 for each level you have attained. For example, if a 7th-level fighter's Constitution increases so the modifier goes from +3 to +4, the fighter's hit point maximum increases by 7.
- Some class features, gained at certain levels, allow you to increase your ability scores (ability score improvements). When increasing ability scores, no ability score may be increased above 20 by these features.

### Multiclassing

- Multiclassing allows a character to gain levels in multiple classes. Doing so lets you mix the abilities of those classes to realize a character concept that might not be reflected in a single class.
- When you multiclass, your levels in all your classes are added together to determine your character level (for example: three levels of wizard and two levels of fighter = 5th-level character). Your character level determines features that depend on overall character level (such as multiclass-based proficiency calculations described elsewhere) and your proficiency bonus, which increases at the levels shown in the Character Advancement table above.
- When your character gains a level, you choose whether that level is in a new class or in one of your existing classes. Choosing a new class means you begin taking levels in that class instead of gaining another level in your current class.
- To qualify for a new class via multiclassing, you must meet the ability score prerequisites for both your current class and the new class. Without the appropriate ability scores to represent aptitude, you cannot multiclass into that class.

#### Multiclassing Prerequisites (Class → Ability Score Minimum)

| Class     | Ability Score Minimum |
|:----------|:---------------------:|
| Barbarian | Strength 13          |
| Bard      | Charisma 13          |
| Cleric    | Wisdom 13            |
| Druid     | Wisdom 13            |
| Fighter   | Strength 13 or Dexterity 13 |
| Monk      | Dexterity 13 and Wisdom 13  |
| Paladin   | Strength 13 and Charisma 13 |
| Ranger    | Dexterity 13 and Wisdom 13  |
| Rogue     | Dexterity 13         |
| Sorcerer  | Charisma 13          |
| Warlock   | Charisma 13          |

(Example: A barbarian who decides to multiclass into druid must have Strength 13 and Wisdom 13.)

## Interactions

- Proficiency bonus: Add together levels from all classes to determine the character level; use the Character Advancement table above to find the proficiency bonus for that character level.
- Hit points and Hit Dice: Each class level grants one Hit Die of that class's type; when calculating hit points gained at each level, use the class you take the level in to determine the Hit Die to roll or the fixed HP value to take. Constitution modifier increases retroactively increase maximum HP by +1 per level for each +1 of the modifier.
- Class features: Many class-specific features and class advancement tables reference class level rather than character level; when multiclassing, consult each class's description to determine which features apply and how they interact with multiclass levels.
- Ability prerequisites: Multiclassing is gated by ability score prerequisites listed in the Multiclassing Prerequisites table; failing to meet these means you cannot take levels in that class.
