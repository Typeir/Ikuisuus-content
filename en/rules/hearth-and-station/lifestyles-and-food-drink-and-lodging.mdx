# Lifestyles and Food, Drink, and Lodging

This page describes the standard lifestyle tiers a character might keep while in settled communities and the costs for common food, drink, and lodging. Lifestyle level determines typical lodging, food, social standing, and incidental services; the Food, Drink, and Lodging table lists item prices that are included in a character's total lifestyle expenses.

## Mechanics

### Lifestyles

- **Wretched.** You have no place to call home and shelter wherever you can. Typical behavior includes sneaking into barns, huddling in old crates, or sleeping wherever possible. A wretched lifestyle presents abundant dangers: violence, disease, hunger, and little to no legal protections. Other wretched people may covet your gear, and you are beneath the notice of most people.

- **Squalid.** You live in a leaky stable, a mud-floored hut just outside town, or a vermin-infested boarding house in the worst part of town. You have shelter from the elements but live in desperate and often violent conditions, in places rife with disease, hunger, and misfortune. You have few legal protections and little social standing.

- **Poor.** A poor lifestyle means going without many comforts available in a stable community. You have simple food and lodgings, threadbare clothing, and unpredictable accommodations that are sufficient but unpleasant. Typical poor-lifestyle occupations include unskilled laborers, costermongers, peddlers, thieves, and mercenaries. You benefit from some legal protections but must contend with violence, crime, and disease.

- **Modest — 1 gp.** A modest lifestyle keeps you out of the slums and ensures you can maintain your equipment. You live in an older part of town renting a room in a boarding house, inn, or temple. You don’t go hungry or thirsty; your living conditions are simple and clean.

- **Comfortable — 2 gp.** Choosing a comfortable lifestyle means you can afford nicer clothing and can easily maintain your equipment. You live in a small cottage in a middle-class neighborhood or a private room at a fine inn. You associate with merchants, skilled tradespeople, and military officers; you have respectable lodgings and likely a small staff of servants.

- **Wealthy — 4 gp.** A wealthy lifestyle reflects money comparable to that of a highly successful merchant or owner of small businesses. You likely live in a spacious home in a good part of town or a comfortable suite at a fine inn. You retain skilled servants and have excellent weapons, armor, and adventuring gear that represent a fortune by most standards.

- **Aristocratic — 10 gp minimum.** Aristocratic denotes a life of plenty and comfort on par with nobility or royalty. You move in circles populated by the most powerful people—politicians, guild leaders, high priests, and nobility. You dine at the best restaurants, retain fashionable servants and retainers, and enjoy privileges that place you above the notice of most people.

Notes on lifestyles:

- Most people at a given lifestyle level have sometimes suffered setbacks (exile, disease, or other misfortune) and may be marked by those events.
- The wealthier a character is, the greater the chance they will be drawn into political intrigue as a pawn or participant.

### Self-sufficiency and Wilderness Living

- Maintaining a given lifestyle while living off the land: Some characters prefer to spend time away from civilization and sustain themselves in the wild by hunting, foraging, repairing their own gear, and living without spending coin. Maintaining this kind of lifestyle does not require spending coin but is time-consuming and requires appropriate skills.

- **Survival skill interaction.** Proficiency in the Survival skill lets a character live at the equivalent of a comfortable lifestyle while sustaining themselves in the wild (hunting, foraging, repairing gear, etc.).

## Interactions

- Lifestyle costs are cumulative with the item prices listed in the Food, Drink, and Lodging table; the table entries are included in total lifestyle expenses.
- Survival and downtime activities can substitute for coin expenditures when a character chooses to live off the land; in such cases the GM adjudicates whether a character’s time and skill checks justify treating expenses as if the character maintained a given lifestyle.
- Social standing from a lifestyle affects how NPCs treat the character and their exposure to political intrigue, favors, and obligations; higher lifestyles often bring both benefits and social risks.
