# Downtime Activities

## Overview

Downtime activities are tasks characters perform between adventures. They require a prescribed number of days of dedicated work to produce their effects; each day counted toward a downtime activity requires at least 8 hours spent on that activity. Days need not be consecutive. The GM may ask what a character does during downtime or declare that time has passed and allow a summary of activities.

## Mechanics

### Downtime Basics

- Each downtime activity requires a certain number of days to complete before a character gains its benefit.
- A day counts if the character spends at least 8 hours on the activity; the days can be nonconsecutive.
- If a character has more days available than an activity requires, the character may continue the same activity for longer or switch to a new downtime activity.
- If the downtime activity desired is not listed in this section, discuss the proposed activity with the GM for adjudication.

### Crafting

You can craft nonmagical objects, including adventuring equipment and works of art.

- Requirement: You must be proficient with the appropriate artisan's tools (or other tool proficiency relevant to the object). You might also need access to special materials or facilities (for example, a forge to create a sword or suit of armor).
- Output per day: For every day of downtime you spend crafting, you can craft one or more items with a total market value not exceeding 5 gp, and you must expend raw materials worth half the total market value.
- Progress for expensive items: If an item you want to craft has a market value greater than 5 gp, you make progress every day in 5 gp increments until you reach the item's market value. In other words, each full day spent crafting accounts for 5 gp of the item's market value toward completion.

Examples and notes:

- Example (large item): A suit of plate armor (market value 1,500 gp) takes 300 days to craft by yourself (1,500 gp ÷ 5 gp per day = 300 days). You must expend raw materials equal to half the market value (750 gp) over the course of crafting.
- Facilities and tools matter: A character proficient with smith's tools needs access to a forge to create a sword or suit of armor; lack of proper facilities may prevent crafting or increase the time or cost at the GM's discretion.
- Declaration of time: When starting a new adventure, the GM might simply declare that a certain amount of time has passed and allow the player to describe in general what their character was doing during that time. At other times, the GM might want to track exact days spent crafting.

### Combining efforts

Multiple characters can combine their efforts toward crafting a single item, provided:

- Each contributing character is proficient with the requisite tools and they are working together in the same place (access to required facilities), and
- Each character contributes the normal per-day crafting progress for a helper: each character contributes 5 gp worth of progress per day spent helping to craft the item.

Example of combined effort:

- Three characters with the requisite tool proficiency and proper facilities can combine to craft a suit of plate armor in 100 days (three characters × 5 gp per day = 15 gp per day toward the item's market value; 1,500 gp ÷ 15 gp per day = 100 days). The total raw-material cost remains half the market value (750 gp) unless the GM rules otherwise.

### Lifestyle Expenses

- Between adventures, you choose a particular lifestyle of life; lifestyles have mechanical effects on how NPCs react and what services are available.
- While crafting, you can maintain a modest lifestyle without having to pay the normal 1 gp per day cost for that lifestyle. Alternatively, you can maintain a comfortable lifestyle at half the normal cost while crafting.
- Living a particular lifestyle has social effects (how individuals and groups react to you), but it does not usually change your character's mechanical capabilities beyond those social and cost effects.

### Practicing a Profession

- You can work between adventures, allowing you to maintain a modest lifestyle without having to pay the normal 1 gp per day associated with it while you practice your profession.
- If you are a member of an organization that can provide gainful employment (for example, a temple or a thieves' guild), you may earn enough to support a comfortable lifestyle instead of merely a modest one.
- This professional benefit persists as long as you continue to practice that profession during downtime.

### Recuperating

You can spend downtime recovering from debilitating injuries, disease, or poison.

- After three days of downtime spent recuperating, make a DC 15 Constitution saving throw.
- On a successful save, you can choose one of the following results:
  - End one effect on you that prevents you from regaining hit points.
  - For the next 24 hours, gain advantage on saving throws against one disease or poison currently affecting you.
- If the save fails, you do not gain those benefits; continue to work with the GM on further recovery options.

## Interactions

- GM adjudication: The GM adjudicates access to facilities, special materials, and whether a proposed downtime activity is allowed or requires modification.
- Multiple systems: Downtime crafting interacts with item cost and market-value rules (use market value to determine crafting time and raw-material cost). Effects that prevent a creature from regaining hit points interact directly with recuperation results (recuperation can end a single such effect on a successful save).
- Time passage: When the GM declares that a long period has passed, players may summarize activities rather than track every day; conversely, the GM may require precise accounting when the details matter.

(If a downtime activity not listed here is desired, discuss it with the GM for a ruling.)
