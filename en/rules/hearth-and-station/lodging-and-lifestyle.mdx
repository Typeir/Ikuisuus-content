# Lodging and Lifestyle

This section defines the standard lifestyle tiers available to characters in settled communities, the daily expense rules for maintaining a lifestyle, and the cost of inn stays at each lifestyle level. Lifestyle choice determines social standing, exposure to risk, and the quality of daily accommodation.

## Mechanics

### Lifestyle Tiers

Characters choose a lifestyle at the start of each period (week or month, as determined by the GM) and pay the listed price per day for the duration of that period. Monthly totals are computed by multiplying the daily price by 30.

- **Wretched (—).** No fixed shelter. Typical behavior includes sneaking into barns, huddling in old crates, or sleeping wherever possible. Abundant dangers: violence, disease, hunger, and little to no legal protections. Other wretched people may covet your gear; you are beneath the notice of most people.

- **Squalid (1 sp/day).** A leaky stable, a mud-floored hut outside town, or a vermin-infested boarding house in the worst part of town. Shelter from the elements, but desperate and often violent conditions rife with disease, hunger, and misfortune. Few legal protections and little social standing.

- **Poor.** Simple food and lodgings, threadbare clothing, and unpredictable accommodations that are sufficient but unpleasant. Typical occupations include unskilled laborers, costermongers, peddlers, thieves, and mercenaries. Some legal protections, but contending with violence, crime, and disease.

- **Modest (1 gp/day).** Keeps you out of the slums and ensures you can maintain your equipment. A room in a boarding house, inn, or temple in an older part of town. Simple and clean; you don't go hungry or thirsty.

- **Comfortable (2 gp/day).** Nicer clothing, easily maintained equipment. A small cottage in a middle-class neighborhood or a private room at a fine inn. You associate with merchants, skilled tradespeople, and military officers, and likely have a small staff of servants.

- **Wealthy (4 gp/day).** Money comparable to a highly successful merchant or small business owner. A spacious home in a good part of town or a comfortable suite at a fine inn. Skilled servants, excellent weapons, armor, and adventuring gear that represent a fortune by most standards.

- **Aristocratic (10 gp minimum/day).** Life of plenty on par with nobility or royalty. You move in circles populated by politicians, guild leaders, high priests, and nobility. Fashionable servants, retainers, the best restaurants, and privileges that place you above the notice of most people.

Notes:

- Most people at a given lifestyle level have sometimes suffered setbacks — exile, disease, or other misfortune — and may be marked by those events.
- The wealthier a character, the greater the chance they will be drawn into political intrigue as a pawn or participant.

### Self-Sufficiency and Wilderness Living

Some characters prefer to sustain themselves in the wild by hunting, foraging, and repairing their own gear rather than spending coin. This is time-consuming and requires appropriate skills.

- Proficiency in the Survival skill lets a character live at the equivalent of a comfortable lifestyle while sustaining themselves in the wild.
- Maintaining this kind of lifestyle does not require spending coin but is not compatible with the pace of normal travel or dungeon-clearing.

### Inn Stay Costs

Inn stay prices are per day and are included in a character's total lifestyle expenses.

| Lifestyle    | Inn Stay (per day) |
| ------------ | :----------------: |
| Squalid      |        7 cp        |
| Poor         |        1 sp        |
| Modest       |        5 sp        |
| Comfortable  |        8 sp        |
| Wealthy      |        2 gp        |
| Aristocratic |        4 gp        |

> Note: the costs listed above are baselines. Local prices may vary by region and season.

## Interactions

- Lifestyle choice can affect social interactions and story outcomes: living lavishly might attract attention (positive or dangerous), while living frugally reduces exposure to thieves and social opportunity.
- Survival and downtime activities can substitute for coin expenditures when a character lives off the land; the GM adjudicates whether a character's time and skill checks justify treating expenses as if the character maintained a given lifestyle.
- Social standing from a lifestyle affects how NPCs treat the character and their exposure to political intrigue, favors, and obligations; higher lifestyles often bring both benefits and social risks.
