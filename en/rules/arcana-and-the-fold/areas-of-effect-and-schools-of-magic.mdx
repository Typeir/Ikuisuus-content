# Areas of Effect and Schools of Magic

## Overview

This entry explains how a spell's area of effect is defined and resolved, and summarizes the schools of magic used to categorize spells. It covers points of origin, how coverage and obstructions interact with areas, attack rolls and saving throws for spells, the specific geometry of common area shapes, and the descriptive role of the schools of magic.

## Mechanics

### Points of Origin and Lines of Effect

- A spell’s effect expands in straight lines from its point of origin. The point of origin is typically a point in space, but some spells specify that a target, creature, or object is the origin.
- You select the spell’s point of origin as specified by the spell. If no unblocked straight line extends from the point of origin to a location within the area of effect, that location is not included in the spell’s area.
- To block one of these imaginary straight lines, an obstruction must provide total cover to the location in question.

### Saving Throws

- When a spell allows a saving throw, the spell specifies which ability the target uses for the save and what happens on a success or failure.
- The DC to resist one of your spells equals 8 + your spellcasting ability modifier + your proficiency bonus + any special modifiers imposed by features, items, or other effects.

### Attack Rolls for Spells

- Some spells require the caster to make an attack roll to determine whether the spell effect hits a target. Most spells that require attack rolls are ranged attacks.
- Your spell attack bonus equals your spellcasting ability modifier + your proficiency bonus, plus any other modifiers that apply.
- You have disadvantage on a ranged attack roll if you are within 5 feet of a hostile creature that can see you and that creature is not incapacitated.

### Area Shapes (detailed descriptions)

- Cone
  - A cone extends in a direction you choose from its point of origin. A cone’s width at a given point along its length is equal to that point’s distance from the point of origin.
  - A cone’s area of effect specifies its maximum length. A cone’s point of origin is not included in the cone’s area of effect unless you decide otherwise.
  - The cone’s point of origin is not included in its area unless the spell says otherwise.

- Cube
  - You select a cube’s point of origin, which lies anywhere on a face of the cubic effect.
  - A cube’s size is expressed as the length of each side. A cube’s point of origin is not included in the cube’s area of effect unless you decide otherwise.

- Cylinder
  - A cylinder’s point of origin is the center of a circle of a particular radius, as given in the spell description. The circle must lie on the ground or at the height of the spell’s effect.
  - The energy in a cylinder expands in straight lines from the point of origin to the perimeter of the circle, forming the base of the cylinder. The spell’s effect then shoots up from the base or down from the top, to a distance equal to the height of the cylinder.
  - A cylinder’s point of origin is included in the cylinder’s area of effect, unless the spell says otherwise.

- Line
  - A line extends from its point of origin in a straight path up to its length and covers an area defined by its width.
  - A line’s point of origin is not included in the line’s area of effect unless the spell says otherwise.

- Sphere
  - You select a sphere’s point of origin, and the sphere extends outward from that point. A sphere’s size is expressed as a radius in feet that extends from the point.
  - A sphere’s point of origin is included in the sphere’s area of effect unless the spell indicates otherwise.

### Inclusion and Exclusion of Origin

- Whether a point of origin is included in an area of effect depends on the shape and the spell’s wording. Cones, cubes, and lines typically exclude the point of origin unless specified otherwise; cylinders and spheres typically include it unless specified otherwise.

### Miscellaneous Spatial Rules

- If a spell’s area of effect has an origin that is a creature or object, use that creature’s or object’s space as the point of origin per the spell’s description.
- Locations that cannot be reached by an unblocked straight line from the point of origin are not affected by the spell’s area.

## Schools of Magic (descriptive)

- Academies of magic group spells into eight categories called the schools of magic. Scholars, particularly wizards, apply these categories to spells as a way to describe how they function; the schools have no independent rules of their own, though some other rules and features refer to the schools.

- Abjuration
  - Abjuration spells are protective in nature. They create magical barriers, negate harmful effects, harm trespassers, or banish creatures to other planes of existence.

- Conjuration
  - Conjuration spells involve the transportation of objects and creatures from one location to another. Some conjurations summon creatures or objects to the caster’s side; others teleport the caster or create objects or effects out of nothing.

- Divination
  - Divination spells reveal information: secrets long forgotten, glimpses of the future, locations of hidden things, the truth behind illusions, or visions of distant people or places.

- Enchantment
  - Enchantment spells affect the minds of others, influencing or controlling their behavior. They can make enemies see the caster as a friend, force creatures to take a course of action, or control another creature like a puppet.

- Evocation
  - Evocation spells manipulate magical energy to produce a desired effect. Some evoke blasts of fire or lightning; others channel positive energy to heal wounds.

- Illusion
  - Illusion spells deceive the senses or minds of others. They cause people to see things that are not there, to miss things that are there, to hear phantom noises, or to remember things that never happened. Some illusions create phantom images or otherwise alter perception.

(Nota bene: the source references that there are eight schools; only the schools described in the source are listed here verbatim.)

## Interactions

- Cover and total cover interact with areas of effect by blocking the imaginary straight lines used to determine whether a location is within the area; only total cover will block those lines.
- Spell saving throw DC and spell attack bonus are used by many other rules and features; calculate them as described above and apply any additional modifiers from class features, magic items, or circumstances.
- The distinction whether an origin is included in an area matters for targeting and for whether creatures occupying the origin are affected; consult the spell text when in doubt.
