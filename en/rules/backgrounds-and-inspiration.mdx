# Backgrounds and Inspiration

## Overview

A background reveals where your character came from, how you became an adventurer, and the character's place in the world. Choosing a background provides concrete mechanical benefits (features, proficiencies, languages, equipment) and suggested roleplaying cues (personality traits, ideals, bonds, flaws) that help define your character.

## Mechanics

### Proficiencies

- Each background grants proficiency in two skills (see "Using Ability Scores").
- Many backgrounds grant proficiency with one or more tools; the specific tools are detailed in the background's equipment section.
- Some backgrounds also allow a character to learn additional languages beyond those given by race. When a background specifies additional languages, record them as part of the background's proficiencies.
- If a character would gain the same proficiency from two different sources, the character can choose a different proficiency of the same kind (skill or tool) instead.

### Gaining Inspiration

- Your GM can award inspiration for a variety of reasons. Typically, GMs award it when a player plays out personality traits, accepts roleplaying drawbacks presented by a flaw or bond, or otherwise portrays the character in a compelling way.
- The exact circumstances and options for earning inspiration are set by the GM; the GM will tell you how you can earn inspiration in the game.
- You either have inspiration or you don't; you can't stockpile multiple "inspirations" for later use.

### Using Inspiration

- If you have inspiration, you can expend it when you make an attack roll, saving throw, or ability check.
- Spending your inspiration gives you advantage on that roll.
- Additionally, if you have inspiration you can reward another player by giving up your inspiration for good roleplaying, clever thinking, or simply doing something exciting in the game. When another player character does something that really contributes to the story in a fun and interesting way, you can give up your inspiration to give that character inspiration.

### Equipment

- Each background provides a package of starting equipment. The equipment package is a concrete list of items granted by the background.
- If you use the optional rule to spend coin on gear, you do not receive the starting equipment package from your background.
- If you spend coin to purchase your starting gear, you cannot also take the equipment package suggested for your class. Choose one or the other per the optional-starting-wealth rules.

### Suggested Characteristics

- A background contains suggested personal characteristics based on the background's concept. These suggestions include lists of personality traits, ideals, bonds, and flaws intended to inspire roleplaying.
- You can select personality traits and other characteristics directly, roll dice to determine them randomly, or use the suggestions as inspiration for characteristics of your own creation.
- A typical background directs you to choose two personality traits, one ideal, one bond, and one flaw.
- The most important question to ask about your background is: what changed? Why did you stop doing whatever your background describes and start adventuring? Where did you get the money to purchase your starting gear, or, if you come from a wealthy background, why don't you have more money? How did you learn the skills of your class? Use these questions to ground the background in your character's story.

### Customizing a Background

- You might want to tweak some features of a background so it better fits your character or the campaign setting.
- To customize a background, you can replace one feature with any other feature, choose any two skills, and choose a total of two tool proficiencies or languages from the sample backgrounds. These substitutions let you adapt the mechanical benefits while preserving the background's narrative role.
- Work with your GM when a suitable feature cannot be found in the listed samples; together you can create a feature that matches the character concept and campaign.

## Example: Acolyte (sample background)

- What sets you apart from ordinary people who share your background? The sample background presented here provides both concrete benefits (features, proficiencies, and languages) and roleplaying suggestions.
- Acolyte description: You have spent your life in the service of a temple to a specific god or pantheon. You act as an intermediary between the realm of the holy and the mortal world, performing sacred rites and offering sacrifices in order to conduct worshipers into the presence of the divine.
- Being an acolyte is not the same thing as channeling divine power; the background describes social role, duties, and training rather than granting spellcasting by itself.

## Interactions

- Background proficiencies stack with other sources of proficiencies. When duplicate proficiencies would occur, choose an alternative of the same kind (skill or tool) rather than gaining no benefit.
- Inspiration interacts with the advantage mechanic: spending inspiration grants advantage on a single attack roll, saving throw, or ability check. Because inspiration is binary (you have it or you do not), timing and choice matter; a character cannot hold multiple inspirations to use later.
- Equipment choices affect starting gear and gold. Using optional starting-wealth rules (spending coin) replaces the background's equipment package and the class's suggested equipment package; confirm with the GM which option is used in the campaign.

## Summary of Player Actions Required When Selecting a Background

- Select the background name and record the background feature(s).
- Record two skill proficiencies granted by the background.
- Record any tool proficiencies and any additional languages granted by the background.
- Take the equipment package for the background or spend starting coin (per optional rule) — not both.
- Choose two personality traits, one ideal, one bond, and one flaw; if desired, roll randomly from the background's suggested lists or create bespoke traits in consultation with the GM.

## Notes for GMs

- Use inspiration as a flexible reward for memorable roleplaying instead of a rigid currency. Define clear, campaign-appropriate triggers for awarding inspiration so players understand what sort of play is rewarded.
- Allow reasonable customization of backgrounds to fit setting-specific cultures, languages, and social structures, but keep the mechanical substitutions consistent (skills, tools, languages, equipment).
