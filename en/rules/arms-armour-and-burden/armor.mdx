# Armor

## Overview

This section collects the rules for wearing armor and using shields: how Armor Class (AC) is determined by armor category, how Dexterity modifies AC, Strength requirements and their movement effects, stealth consequences, and short descriptions of common armor types.

## Mechanics

- Armor categories: light, medium, heavy. Each category defines a base AC calculation and how (or whether) your Dexterity modifier applies.

- Light armor: made from supple, thin materials and favors agile adventurers. When you wear light armor, you add your full Dexterity modifier to the base number from the armor type to determine your Armor Class.

- Medium armor: offers more protection than light armor but impairs movement more. When you wear medium armor, you add your Dexterity modifier to the base number from your armor type to determine your Armor Class, to a maximum of +2.

- Heavy armor: of all the armor categories, heavy armor offers the best protection. Heavy armor does not let you add your Dexterity modifier to your Armor Class, but it also does not penalize you if your Dexterity modifier is negative.

- Strength requirement / speed penalty: If the Armor table shows a Strength score (for example, "Str 13" or "Str 15") in the Strength column for an armor type, the armor reduces the wearer’s speed by 10 feet unless the wearer has a Strength score equal to or higher than the listed score.

- Stealth: If the Armor table shows "Disadvantage" in the Stealth column for an armor type, the wearer has disadvantage on Dexterity (Stealth) checks while wearing that armor.

- Shields: A shield is made from wood or metal and is carried in one hand. Wielding a shield increases your Armor Class by 2. You can benefit from only one shield at a time.


### Armor type descriptions (text reproduced/paraphrased from source)

- Padded. Padded armor consists of quilted layers of cloth and batting. (Light armor.)

- Leather. The breastplate and shoulder protectors of this armor are made of leather that has been stiffened by being boiled in oil. The rest of the armor is made of softer and more flexible materials.

- Studded leather. Made from tough but flexible leather, studded leather is reinforced with close-set rivets or spikes.

- Ring Mail. This armor is leather with heavy rings sewn into it. The rings help reinforce the armor against blows from swords and axes. Ring mail is inferior to chain mail, and it's usually worn only by those who can't afford better armor.

- Chain Mail. Made of interlocking metal rings, chain mail includes a layer of quilted fabric worn underneath the mail to prevent chafing and to cushion the impact of blows. The suit includes gauntlets.

- Scale Mail. This armor consists of a coat and leggings (and perhaps a separate skirt) of leather covered with overlapping pieces of metal, much like the scales of a fish. The suit includes gauntlets.

- Breastplate. This armor consists of a fitted metal chest piece worn with supple leather. Although it leaves the legs and arms relatively unprotected, this armor provides good protection for the wearer’s vital organs while leaving the wearer relatively unencumbered.

- Half-Plate. Half plate consists of shaped metal plates that cover most of the wearer’s body. It does not include leg protection beyond simple greaves that are attached with leather straps.

- Splint. This armor is made of narrow vertical strips of metal riveted to a backing of leather that is worn over cloth padding. Flexible chain mail protects the joints.

- Chain Shirt. Made of interlocking metal rings, a chain shirt is worn between layers of clothing or leather. This armor offers modest protection to the wearer’s upper body and allows the sound of the rings to be somewhat noticeable.

- Hide. This crude armor consists of thick furs and pelts. It is commonly worn by barbarian tribes, evil humanoids, and other folk who lack access to the tools and materials needed to create better armor.

- Plate. Plate consists of shaped, interlocking metal plates to cover the entire body. A suit of plate includes gauntlets, heavy leather boots, a visored helmet, and thick layers of padding underneath the armor. Buckles and straps distribute the weight over the body.

## Interactions

- Dex and armor category interaction: Light armor uses full Dexterity modifier; medium armor caps the Dexterity contribution at +2; heavy armor ignores Dexterity modifiers (positive or negative) to AC.

- Movement / Strength interaction: Failing an armor’s Strength requirement imposes a 10-foot speed penalty. This interacts with any feature that modifies speed (boots, spells, class features): apply the armor penalty to the character’s total speed after calculating other modifiers.

- Stealth: When armor imposes stealth disadvantage, that disadvantage applies to any check that uses Dexterity (Stealth), including checks made during ambushes, hiding, or other stealth actions.

- Shields: A character using a shield gains +2 to AC; features that require both hands or impose shield restrictions conflict with wielding a shield. You may only benefit from one shield at a time.

- Proficiency: Only creatures or characters proficient with a given armor type can wear it effectively; lack of proficiency may impose penalties determined by the governing ruleset (consult character proficiency rules).
