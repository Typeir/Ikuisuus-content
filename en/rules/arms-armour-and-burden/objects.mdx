# Objects

These rules govern how inanimate, destructible objects interact with attacks and effects. They cover assigning Armor Class and hit points to objects, damage thresholds for large structures, how to treat Huge and Gargantuan objects, and which damage types affect objects more or less effectively.

## General Principles

### Huge and Gargantuan Objects

Normal weapons and small tools are often of little use against many Huge and Gargantuan objects (for example, a colossal statue or a towering column). Given enough time and the right tools, characters can destroy nearly any destructible object, but time, method, and tool choice matter. If time is a factor, assign an Armor Class and hit points to the object and track progress. Alternatively, divide a very large object into Large-or-smaller sections and track each section's hit points separately; destroying one section can ruin or topple the entire object. For example, a Gargantuan statue of a humanoid might topple when one of its Large legs is reduced to 0 hit points. Use common sense when deciding what weapon or force is acting against an object (for instance, a sword is likely to break before cutting through a stone wall).

When characters need to saw through ropes, shatter a window, burn a tapestry, smash a vampire's coffin, or chip at a massive boulder, choose the most appropriate approach (tools, fire, magic). A torch can burn a Huge tapestry; an earthquake spell can reduce a colossus to rubble.

### Objects and Damage Types

- Objects are immune to poison and psychic damage.
- Some damage types are more effective against particular substances. For example, bludgeoning damage works well for smashing fragile or brittle things (crystal, glass, stone), while slashing or cutting damage works better for cutting rope, cloth, or leather. Paper and cloth objects might be vulnerable to fire and lightning. A pick can chip away at stone but cannot effectively cut down a tree.
- The GM may assign resistances, vulnerabilities, or immunities to specific objects where appropriate.

### Armor Class

An object’s Armor Class is a measure of how difficult it is to deal damage to it when striking it (the object has no ability to dodge). Use the Object Armor Class table below for suggested AC values for common substances, or assign an AC based on the object's construction and circumstances.

Object Armor Class (suggested values)

|                 Substance | AC  |
| ------------------------: | :-: |
|        Cloth, paper, rope | 11  |
|       Crystal, glass, ice | 13  |
|                Wood, bone | 15  |
|                     Stone | 17  |
|               Iron, steel | 19  |
|            Ancient alloys | 21  |
| Near-unbreakable material | 23  |

### Damage Threshold

Big, sturdy objects such as castle walls often have extra resilience represented by a damage threshold. An object with a damage threshold is immune to all damage from an attack or effect unless that single attack or effect deals an amount of damage equal to or greater than the object's damage threshold. If an attack deals damage equal to or greater than the threshold, the object takes damage as normal. Any damage that fails to meet or exceed the object's damage threshold is superficial and does not reduce the object's hit points.

The GM decides whether an object has a damage threshold and sets an appropriate value based on the object's size, construction, and circumstances.

### Hit Points

An object’s hit points measure how much damage it can take before losing its structural integrity. Resilient objects have more hit points than fragile ones. Large objects generally have more hit points than small ones, unless breaking a small part of the object is as effective as breaking the whole thing.

Object Hit Points (suggested values)

|  Size  | Fragile (hp) | Fragile (dice) | Resilient (hp) | Resilient (dice) |
| :----: | :----------: | :------------: | :------------: | :--------------: |
|  Tiny  |      2       |      1d4       |       5        |       2d4        |
| Small  |      3       |      1d6       |       10       |       3d6        |
| Medium |      4       |      1d8       |       18       |       4d8        |
| Large  |      5       |      1d10      |       27       |       5d10       |

Use these suggested values as a baseline. The GM can adjust hit points to reflect an object's construction, magical reinforcement, or special materials.

## Interactions

- When determining whether a character can damage an object with a given weapon or effect, use common sense and the object's listed defenses (AC, hit points, damage threshold, resistances).
- If an object is composed of many discrete parts (for example, a vehicle, a wagon, or a multi-piece statue), consider dividing it into sections and tracking each section’s hit points separately; destroying a key section can render the entire object inoperative or cause it to collapse.
- A tool or weapon ill-suited to the task (for example, a sword against a stone wall) might break, or the attack might be insufficient to meet a damage threshold; apply tool durability or forced weapon breakage at the GM’s discretion.
- Spells and area effects may bypass ordinary limitations (for example, fire can destroy cloth more effectively than bludgeoning attacks; a powerful area-effect spell can meet or exceed a high damage threshold in a single hit).

Use these rules and the suggested tables as guidance; the GM should adapt values and rulings to the fiction and tactical situation at the table.
