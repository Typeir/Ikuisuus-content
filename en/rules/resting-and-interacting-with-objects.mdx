# Resting and Interacting with Objects

## Overview

This rule describes how rest, food, and water affect characters, how short and long rests restore hit points and Hit Dice, and how characters interact with and damage objects in the environment. It reproduces the mechanical details required to adjudicate exhaustion, healing, and object interactions.

## Eating and Going Without Food

Eating half a pound of food in a day counts as half a day without food.

- A character can go without food for a number of days equal to 3 + the character's Constitution modifier (minimum 1).
- At the end of each day beyond that limit, the character automatically suffers one level of exhaustion.
- A normal day of eating resets the count of days without food to zero.

## Water

| Condition | Water needed per day |
|---|---:|
| Normal weather | 1 gallon |
| Hot weather | 2 gallons |

- If a character drinks only half the required water for the day, that character must succeed on a DC 15 Constitution saving throw or suffer one level of exhaustion at the end of the day.
- If a character has access to even less water than half the requirement (effectively none), the character automatically suffers one level of exhaustion at the end of the day.
- If the character already has one or more levels of exhaustion, suffering the above effects causes two levels of exhaustion instead of one.

## Short Rest

- A short rest is a period of downtime at least 1 hour long, during which a character does nothing more strenuous than eating, drinking, reading, and tending to wounds.
- During a short rest, a character can spend one or more Hit Dice, up to the character's maximum number of Hit Dice.
- For each Hit Die spent:
  - The player rolls the Hit Die and adds the character's Constitution modifier to the roll.
  - The character regains hit points equal to the total from that roll.
  - After each die is rolled, the player can decide whether to spend an additional Hit Die.
- Some spent Hit Dice are regained by finishing a long rest (see Long Rest).

## Long Rest

- A long rest is a period of extended downtime at least 8 hours long, during which a character sleeps or performs light activity: reading, talking, eating, or standing watch for no more than 2 hours.
- If the rest is interrupted by a period of strenuous activity — at least 1 hour of walking, fighting, casting spells, or similar adventuring activity — the characters must begin the rest again to gain any benefit from it.
- At the end of a long rest, a character regains all lost hit points and regains spent Hit Dice as explained below.
- A character can't benefit from more than one long rest in a 24-hour period.
- A character must have at least 1 hit point at the start of the rest to gain its benefits.

## Regaining Hit Dice

- When a long rest is finished, a character regains spent Hit Dice up to a number of dice equal to half of the character's total number of Hit Dice (minimum of one die).
- For example, if a character has eight Hit Dice, finishing a long rest restores four spent Hit Dice.
- These regained Hit Dice are added back to the character's pool of available Hit Dice for future short rests.

## Interacting with Objects

- A character's interaction with objects in the environment is often simple to resolve: the player describes what the character is doing (for example, pulling a lever), and the GM describes what, if anything, happens.
- The GM decides whether the action requires a roll and which ability and DC apply. Example: a rusted lever that must be forced might require a Strength check. The GM sets the DC based on the difficulty of the task.
- Example situations:
  - Pulling a lever might raise a portcullis, cause a room to flood, or open a secret door in a nearby wall.
  - If a lever is rusted in position, the GM can call for a Strength check to see whether the character can wrench it into place.

## Damaging and Breaking Objects

- Characters can damage objects with weapons and spells. Objects are vulnerable to physical and magical attacks much like creatures can be, but with the following clarifications:
  - Objects are immune to poison and psychic damage.
  - Objects always fail Strength and Dexterity saving throws, and they are immune to effects that require other saving throws.
  - The GM determines an object's Armor Class and hit points and might decide that certain objects have resistance or immunity to particular kinds of attacks (for example, it's hard to cut a rope with a club).
- When an object drops to 0 hit points, it breaks.
- A character can also attempt a Strength check to break an object; the GM sets the DC for the check.

## Interactions and Adjudication Notes

- Players should describe the intended interaction with an object clearly; the GM adjudicates consequences and required checks.
- The GM should apply the above rules for exhaustion, water, and food strictly: count days without food, apply the DC 15 Constitution save for insufficient water, apply exhaustion levels as specified, and enforce long/short rest timing rules.
- When recovering hit points during short rests, track Hit Dice spent and ensure long rests restore up to half the total Hit Dice (minimum 1) as described above.
