# Death and Dying

## Overview

This section governs what happens when a creature is reduced to 0 hit points, how death saving throws work, the effects of massive damage and instant death, how temporary hit points interact with dying, how to stabilize a creature, and the optional "knock out" choice an attacker can make.

## Mechanics

### Falling Unconscious

- If damage reduces you to 0 hit points and fails to kill you outright, you fall unconscious. Unconscious creatures follow the same death-saving-throw rules as player characters.
- Unconsciousness ends if you regain any hit points.

### Massive Damage and Instant Death

- Massive damage can kill you instantly. If damage reduces you to 0 hit points and the remaining damage equals or exceeds your hit point maximum, you die instantly.
- Example: A cleric with a maximum of 12 hit points currently has 6 hit points. If she takes 18 damage from an attack, she is reduced to 0 hit points and 12 damage remains. Because the remaining damage equals her hit point maximum, the cleric dies.
- Most GMs treat monsters as dying the instant they drop to 0 hit points rather than falling unconscious; this is a table/GM decision and not part of the forced player rules.

### Death Saving Throws

- Whenever you start your turn with 0 hit points, you must make a special saving throw, called a death saving throw, to determine whether you creep closer to death or hang on to life.
- A death saving throw uses no ability score. Roll a d20:
  - 10 or higher: success.
  - 9 or lower: failure.
- Track successes and failures separately. They do not need to be consecutive; keep track of both until you collect three of a kind.
  - Three successes: you become stable (see "Stabilized" below).
  - Three failures: you die.
- Rolling a 1 on the d20 counts as two failures.
- Rolling a 20 on the d20 immediately regains 1 hit point.

### Damage at 0 Hit Points

- If you take any damage while you have 0 hit points, you suffer a death saving throw failure.
- If the damage is from a critical hit while you have 0 hit points, you suffer two failures instead of one.
- If damage reduces you to 0 hit points and the remaining damage equals or exceeds your hit point maximum, you die outright (see "Massive Damage and Instant Death").

### Stabilized Creatures

- On your third success on death saving throws, you become stable. A stable creature is not dying and does not make death saving throws.
- A stable creature regains 1 hit point after 1d4 hours.
- If a stable creature takes any damage, it stops being stable and must start making death saving throws again.
- When you regain any hit points or become stable, reset your number of successes and failures to zero.

### Stabilizing a Creature (Using an Action)

- You can use your action to administer first aid to an unconscious creature and attempt to stabilize it.
- This attempt requires a successful DC 10 Wisdom (Medicine) check. If successful, the creature is stabilized and no longer makes death saving throws; it still regains 1 hit point after 1d4 hours if not healed sooner.
- If healing is available, the best way to save a creature with 0 hit points is to restore hit points by magical or mundane healing; stabilizing only prevents further death saves until healing can occur.

### Knocking a Creature Out

- Sometimes an attacker wants to incapacitate a foe rather than deal a killing blow. When an attacker reduces a creature to 0 hit points with a melee attack, the attacker can choose to knock the creature out instead of killing it.
- If the attacker chooses this option, the creature falls unconscious and is stable.
- The attacker must make this choice the instant the damage is dealt.

### Temporary Hit Points

- Some spells and abilities grant temporary hit points to a creature. Temporary hit points are a separate pool of hit points that protect you from damage; they are not actual hit points.
- When you have temporary hit points and take damage, the temporary hit points are lost first. Any leftover damage then carries over to your normal hit points.
  - Example: If you have 5 temporary hit points and take 7 damage, you lose the 5 temporary hit points and then take 2 damage to your normal hit points.
- Temporary hit points do not restore actual hit points and cannot be healed away; they function only as a buffer.
- Temporary hit points do not stack. If you have temporary hit points and receive more of them, you decide whether to keep the ones you have or to gain the new ones.
- Having temporary hit points does not restore you to consciousness. If you have 0 hit points, receiving temporary hit points does not make you conscious nor does it remove the need to make death saving throws; however, while you have temporary hit points they absorb future damage first.
- Temporary hit points can cause your effective total (actual hit points plus temporary hit points) to exceed your hit point maximum; that is permitted but does not change your maximum or heal you.

## Examples and Clarifications

- Rolling a 1 on a death save: counts as two failures.
- Rolling a 20 on a death save: you regain 1 hit point and are no longer unconscious.
- A stable creature that is not healed will regain 1 hit point after 1d4 hours.
- If a stable creature takes damage, it immediately loses stability and must start making death saving throws again.

## Interactions

- Spells, special abilities, and features that restore hit points immediately remove the need for death saving throws when they restore at least 1 hit point.
- Effects that grant temporary hit points interact with damage while at 0 hit points by absorbing damage first, but they do not grant consciousness.
- The GM may choose to treat monsters differently (for example, having them die at 0 hit points) for pacing or dramatic effect; such rulings alter the presented player-focused rules but are a table decision.
